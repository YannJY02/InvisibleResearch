# Direct interdisciplinary explanations of uneven scholarly visibility

Status: targeted open-discovery memo for
[Search beyond Khanna for direct interdisciplinary explanations](https://github.com/YannJY02/InvisibleResearch/issues/77).
It identifies source-specific full-text reading candidates. It is not a
Literature Evidence Note, a literature-review synthesis, or evidence that any
candidate mechanism is established.

## Question and boundary

Which English-language studies in interdisciplinary venues and adjacent fields
offer direct explanations of uneven scholarly visibility or recognition that
are not already represented by the Khanna-derived paths?

For this search, an explanation was **direct** only when the source made a
scholarly outcome part of its object of study: publication or funding
evaluation, citation, diffusion of scientific ideas, recognition of a scholar
or contribution, or the devaluation of scholarship inside academia. A concept
borrowed from another domain without being tested or developed for scholarship
was a loose analogy and was excluded.

The search preserved the existing distinction among discoverability, citation
circulation, recognition or evaluation, and non-academic use. It also treated
the Khanna-derived memo as a coverage check, not as a preferred causal model.

## Search and verification protocol

- **Discovery date:** 2026-07-20
- **Discovery service:** AnySearch `academic.search`, with English queries and
  article-oriented retrieval. Results were screened by title and abstract.
- **Identity verification:** Crossref was queried by DOI for the retained core
  set. All ten DOI records matched the recorded title, authors, year, and venue.
- **Access triage:** OpenAlex open-access locations and local Zotero attachments
  were used only to route later full-text work. An OA flag was not treated as
  evidence that a paper had been read.
- **Citation-context check:** Scite reports were checked for the core set. They
  surfaced both supporting and contrasting citation contexts for several
  candidates, especially Tomkins et al. (2017), Wang et al. (2015, 2017),
  Dworkin et al. (2020), Wang et al. (2013), and Evans (2008). Those counts are
  discovery signals, not quality scores.
- **Zotero check:** Bol et al. (2018) and Wang, Song, and Barabási (2013) were
  already present with linked PDF attachments. Evans (2008), whose public full
  text could not be confirmed, was added as bibliographic item `6V6PABSK` with
  no attachment. No source was admitted to the Literature Evidence Note corpus.

### Inclusion criteria

1. English-language journal article or high-quality review/conceptual article.
2. Directly explains a scholarly visibility or recognition outcome.
3. Adds a mechanism, boundary condition, or counter-explanation not already
   explicit in the Khanna-derived path memo.
4. Verifiable DOI and bibliographic identity.

### Exclusion criteria

1. Merely documents an unequal distribution without proposing or testing an
   explanation.
2. Uses attention, status, or inequality outside scholarship only as analogy.
3. Repeats index selection, metadata readiness, linguistic hierarchy, local
   orientation, or citation dispersion without a new mechanism.
4. Commentary that contributes neither a clear concept nor evidence.
5. Unverifiable or mismatched bibliographic identity.

## Newly surfaced explanatory paths

### 1. Status cues and cumulative advantage are conditional, not automatic

This path explains recognition as a feedback process in which an early award,
funding decision, famous name, or prestigious affiliation becomes information
for later evaluators. It differs from the Khanna path about assessment policies:
the immediate mechanism is an audience using prior status as a quality cue under
uncertainty, followed by additional resources or reduced reapplication by early
losers.

- Bol et al. (2018) compare applicants just above and below a grant threshold.
  Their abstract attributes the later funding gap partly to the first award and
  partly to unsuccessful applicants applying less often. The behavioral term is
  a direct counter to an all-gatekeeper explanation.
- Tomkins et al. (2017) experimentally compare single- and double-blind reviews
  at one computer-science conference. Their abstract reports preferential
  bidding and higher acceptance recommendations for famous authors and top
  institutions when identities are visible.
- Morgan et al. (2018) connect faculty-hiring prestige networks to the spread of
  five computer-science topics. Their abstract reports that network origin can
  change diffusion even when idea quality is held comparable in the model.

**Limit:** these studies cover funding, one conference, and North American
computer science. They do not establish a universal status effect or show that
prestige explains journal noncoverage in the OJS population.

### 2. Epistemic exclusion links disciplinary norms and identity to scholarly devaluation

This path treats invisibility as a judgment that some scholarship is
illegitimate, unintelligible, or outside a field's canonical concerns. It is
more specific than geographic or linguistic hierarchy because it names formal
and informal gatekeeping within a discipline and allows disciplinary and
identity biases to interact.

- Settles et al. (2020) use epistemic exclusion and visibility theory to explain
  psychology's resistance to intersectionality through mainstream-journal
  exclusion and repeated misrepresentation. This is a conceptual candidate;
  the full text must be read to separate documented evidence from theoretical
  interpretation.
- Dworkin et al. (2020) model expected gender composition in neuroscience
  reference lists and examine author-team characteristics associated with the
  imbalance. It offers a citation-selection mechanism at article level rather
  than treating workforce composition alone as the outcome.
- Wu (2024) is a necessary counter-reading. Its review distinguishes per-paper,
  per-author, and reference-ratio gaps and argues that lower career citation
  totals for women are driven mainly by lower publication output rather than a
  per-article citation penalty. That claim directly contests a simple citation-
  bias interpretation and requires full-text appraisal.

**Limit:** identity categories, disciplines, and outcome denominators differ
across these sources. Apparent contradiction may be a unit-of-analysis problem
rather than mutually exclusive evidence.

### 3. Novelty and cognitive distance can delay recognition

This path explains weak early visibility through the content and audience of a
paper. Work that combines distant knowledge or departs from established paths
may take longer to find a receptive community, while also having greater upside
if it is eventually recognized.

- Wang, Veugelers, and Stephan (2017) report that highly novel work has greater
  variance in citation outcomes and a longer delay before highly cited outcomes
  emerge. The paper is a direct warning against equating low early citation with
  low long-run value.
- Wang, Thijs, and Glänzel (2015) separate variety, balance, and disparity in
  interdisciplinarity. Their abstract reports different short- and long-term
  citation associations, including early penalties for some dimensions.

**Limit:** both are observational bibliometric studies. Content measures may
capture field composition or journal selection, and citation delay does not by
itself reveal unfair evaluation.

### 4. Attention dynamics can create inequality without a discrete exclusion decision

This path explains uneven citation through cumulative attention, intrinsic
paper fitness, aging, and search behavior. It supplies a genuine competing
explanation: even in the absence of a discriminatory gatekeeper, early attention
and time-dependent decay can separate citation trajectories.

- Wang, Song, and Barabási (2013) model long-run citations as a combination of
  preferential attachment, paper-specific fitness, and aging. The model must be
  read as a system-level mechanism, not proof that social exclusion is absent.
- Evans (2008) argues that electronic searching and hyperlink following narrow
  the range of journals and articles cited while favoring recent and prevailing
  work. Scite surfaced both supporting and contrasting contexts, so this source
  should be paired with the already-open Lariviere et al. counter-reading rather
  than used alone.

**Limit:** the Evans version of record was not available in this pass. Its
abstract is enough to justify acquisition, not a source note or a settled claim.

## Answer and limits

The search found direct explanations beyond Khanna in four areas: conditional
status cues and cumulative advantage; epistemic exclusion and identity-
conditioned gatekeeping; novelty or cognitive distance producing delayed
recognition; and attention dynamics involving network prestige, preferential
attachment, aging, and search behavior. It also found two counter-explanations
that must stay live: unsuccessful applicants may self-select out of later
competition, and career-level gender citation gaps may arise mainly from
publication-output differences even where per-paper citation penalties are not
observed.

This is not conceptual saturation. The retained set is concentrated in
quantitative science-of-science, North American or European venues, and gender-
focused identity research. Race, disability, Indigenous scholarship, humanities
recognition, book-centered fields, policy use, and professional uptake remain
underrepresented. Full-text readings may reject candidates whose abstracts
overstate causal identification or whose constructs do not transfer beyond one
discipline.
