## Question and reading basis

## Working distinctions

Khanna et al. move among several outcomes that later discovery must not collapse:

- **Discoverability or index inclusion:** whether a journal or work can be found
  in a named database.
- **Citation circulation:** whether work is cited, and within or beyond which
  journal or language communities.
- **Recognition or evaluation:** whether authors, journals, or knowledge are
  treated as credible, prestigious, or reward-worthy.
- **Policy or professional use:** whether research affects decisions and
  practice outside citation systems.

These are search-facing distinctions, not settled repository glossary terms.
Evidence about one outcome cannot stand in for another.

## Competing explanations to preserve

The next searches should seek evidence for these alternatives rather than use
them as rhetorical caveats:

1. **Editorial selection and integrity:** selective databases may partly reflect
   editorial standards, peer-review practices, stability, or documented
   integrity rather than only systemic exclusion.
2. **Identifier and metadata readiness:** missing ISSNs, weak metadata, blocked
   crawling, and inconsistent journal URLs may produce noncoverage without a
   recognition decision.
3. **Voluntary orientation:** authors and journals may prioritize local
   audiences, teaching, policy, or professional communities over international
   citation systems.
4. **Audience and language network size:** lower cross-system citation may arise
   from smaller or more locally bounded readerships even when within-community
   use is substantial.
5. **Journal composition:** age, activity, field, publication volume, and
   article type may explain part of the coverage or citation difference.
6. **Measurement and sample construction:** optional Beacon transmission, the
   five-items threshold, ISSN-dependent subsets, domain-level Google Scholar
   matching, and asynchronous index snapshots can create apparent differences.
7. **System-level dispersion:** citations may be becoming less concentrated
   overall even while particular regions, languages, or infrastructures remain
   disadvantaged.

- `(research assessment OR journal evaluation) AND (indexing OR citation) AND (Global South OR peripheral)`
- `(metadata interoperability OR scholarly infrastructure OR identifier coverage) AND journal visibility`
- `(language bias OR multilingual scholarship) AND (index coverage OR citation circulation OR recognition)`
- `(predatory label OR journal integrity) AND (open access OR Global South) AND scholarly publishing`
- `(regional journal OR multilingual journal) AND (citation network OR policy citation OR professional use)`

The open search should sample competing traditions deliberately: journal
selection and integrity, author behavior and incentives, infrastructure and
metadata, language and audience networks, political economy, and system-level
citation dispersion.

## Limits and next decision

- A citation by Khanna et al. establishes relevance to their argument, not the
  cited source's quality or support for that argument.
- OpenAlex OA metadata is a discovery aid and may be stale; the source must be
  opened and read before admission.
- The source list is concentrated in scholarly communication and sociology and
  is not conceptually saturated. Broader interdisciplinary discovery remains
  necessary.
- No source in this memo supports collapsing technical discoverability,
  citation, prestige, and social use into one measure of “visibility.”

The next takeable work is source-specific full-text reading for the first wave,
plus the independent search beyond Khanna. Conceptual saturation cannot be
judged until both streams have produced notes and a later counter-perspective
audit has tested what they missed.
