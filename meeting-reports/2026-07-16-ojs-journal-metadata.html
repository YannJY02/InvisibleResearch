Why this matters

Two upstream questions guide this work.

The meeting is a response to the shared project tracker. The metadata task is not an end in itself. It prepares the evidence needed to study invisible research.

Upstream issue #6

OJS Journal Metadata

Build an automated pipeline. Merge PKP journal rows with metadata from OpenAlex and, only when useful, other sources.

Open issue #6
→
Prepare issue #5

Predictors of invisible research

Map source-specific invisibility. Then study whether language, country, DOI activity, past citations, or other features are linked to inclusion.

Open issue #5
Need 1

A clear outcome

“Invisible” cannot start as one loose binary label. OpenAlex coverage, no ISSN, and an ambiguous match are different states.

Need 2

A stable cohort

We need to know which rows can enter a match before comparing groups or training a model.

Need 3

Traceable evidence

Every result must link back to a pinned input, a clear rule, and a reproducible output.

Show the exact preparation needs from upstream issue #5
  • Map invisibility across sources such as Web of Science and Scopus.
  • Study possible predictors such as language, country, DOI, and past citations.
  • Check which variables are available in Beacon data.
  • Give each journal a clear inclusion outcome.
  • Only after those steps, test a model such as XGBoost and use SHAP to inspect what is linked to inclusion.
What I want the group to remember

The pipeline answers issue #6. Its main value is to make a careful version of issue #5 possible.

02

What Khanna et al. show

The hidden system is already large and diverse.

I read the full paper: Khanna, Ball, Alperin, and Willinsky (2022), “Recalibrating the scope of scholarly publishing.” These are the seven findings that matter most for our project.

01 · SCALE

25,671

active OJS journals

This sample alone makes a world estimate of about 30,000 journals too small. The paper does not claim a complete world count.

02 · PLACE

136

countries

81.6% fall in Global South countries under the paper’s World Bank grouping. Indonesia and Brazil are the largest groups.

03 · LANGUAGE

60

detected languages

49.7% mainly publish in English. 48.3% publish in more than one language. The estimate uses ISSN-verified journals.

04 · FIELD & MODEL

84.2%

estimated OA diamond

OJS journals span social science, STEM, and humanities. The OA diamond figure is inferred from earlier survey values, not measured in the 2020 Beacon sample.

05 · INDEXING

1.2 → 88.3%

coverage depends on the source

Reported coverage runs from 1.2% in Web of Science to 88.3% in Google Scholar. OpenAlex is 63.8% in the paper’s own cohort.

06 · QUALITY CLAIMS

1.0–1.4%

overlap with two predatory lists

Low overlap does not prove quality. It does show why we should not dismiss the whole publishing stratum as suspect.

07 · BOUNDARY

recenter

not “decolonization achieved”

Recognition can move the assumed center of publishing. The authors also warn that database inclusion is not the return of Indigenous land and life.

Show the paper’s data and methods

Data and scope

  • Public 2020 PKP Beacon data.
  • Active rule: at least five items in 2020.
  • 25,671 journals; 36.5% of 70,214 PKP beacons.
  • ISSN-dependent subsets include 22,809 journals for index matches and 22,561 for language.
  • Index snapshots span January 2021 to June 2022.

Methods

  • Country from ISSN/MARC, country-code domain, and host-IP signals.
  • Language from up to 100 titles and abstracts per journal, plus checks and 478 semimanual corrections.
  • Discipline from five recent titles and abstracts, translated into English and classified to one ANZSRC field.
  • Eleven indexes, directories, and lists matched by ISSN, URL, or domain.
  • Preliminary Google Scholar work used about ten returned articles per OJS domain.
Show figure conflicts and review cautions
  • The abstract reports 5.8 million cumulative items. The body reports 5.4 million.
  • The abstract reports 5.7% Scopus coverage. Table 3 reports 7.2% for its 22,809-journal subset.
  • Country can reflect a hosting server or data center rather than the journal’s organization.
  • English may be overestimated because titles and abstracts are often translated.
  • The sample covers active, transmitting OJS installations. It is not all OJS journals.
  • All four authors are linked to PKP, the developer of OJS. The paper reports this interest.
What I want the group to remember

Index absence is not journal absence. It is a source-specific view of a large publishing system.

03

My understanding and inspiration

I turned the reading into four design rules.

The paper did not give me a ready-made pipeline. It changed how I think the pipeline should treat missing data, identity, comparison, and the next research question.

The paper says

Selective sources hide different parts of publishing.

→
I apply

Keep source-specific outcomes.

Do not collapse OpenAlex, Scopus, Web of Science, or Crossref absence into one generic failure.

The paper shows

ISSN-dependent results use smaller cohorts.

→
I apply

Separate identifier access from index coverage.

A row with no ISSN cannot be called “not covered by OpenAlex” under an ISSN-only route.

The paper warns

Location and language fields are inferred.

→
I apply

Use careful names and caveats.

PKP-inferred country is useful for description. It is not an authoritative publisher location.

The paper motivates

Coverage differences may follow social and technical strata.

→
I apply

Describe first; model later.

The first profile checks patterns. It does not call them predictors or causes.

What I want the group to remember

My main lesson is not “add more databases.” It is “define each missingness state before we compare or model it.”

What I prepared and built

One strict route, with every row kept.

The code is long because it handles input checks, API recovery, cache files, matching evidence, and reports. Its main logic is simple.

01Verify inputCheck MD5, required columns, row count, and row identity.
02Keep OJS rowsFilter application to OJS. Keep all 86,282 rows.
03Clean ISSNsSplit values, normalize them, check the checksum, and deduplicate tokens.
04Ask OpenAlexSend at most 100 ISSNs per list call. Cache each response.
05Match locallyCompare each token with every Source ISSN. Keep all candidates.
06Classify resultOne journal Source = unique. Many IDs = ambiguous. No ID = unmatched.
07Left join & reportReturn to every PKP row. Write coverage, review, cost, and quality evidence.

Show the technical contract and safety checks

Input

  • Dataverse V6.0 beacon.csv, file ID 13173372.
  • Original CSV parser supports multiline quoting.
  • repository_name can be empty.
  • stats_id and ISSN are not row keys.
  • A new annual release becomes a new Artifact Version and must be profiled again.

Output

  • Every source field carries match route and provenance.
  • Non-journal candidates and conflicts are not hidden.
  • admin_email is excluded from joined files and reports.
  • Title fallback is opt-in, bounded, and never equal to exact ISSN.
  • OPENALEX_API_KEY is required for live calls and never logged.
  • Only transient and rate errors are retried, with bounded backoff.
What I want the group to remember

The pipeline does not force a match. It makes uncertainty visible and keeps the original PKP population intact.

08

What the results do not show

Five lines I will not cross.

No causal claim

Country, activity, duration, and DOAJ evidence are not proven causes.

They are separate descriptive views. They may also overlap with each other.

No quality claim

Unmatched does not mean low quality or “predatory.”

It means no unique journal Source was found by this exact-ISSN route.

No identity repair

The strict route does not solve title or ISSN history.

Some unmatched and ambiguous rows may need ISSN-L, title history, or homepage review.

No universal invisibility

OpenAlex noncoverage is one outcome.

Scopus, Web of Science, Crossref, citations, language, and DOI infrastructure describe other forms of visibility.

No authority promotion

This remains Exploratory Analysis.

No Candidate Version, Designation Event, or Paper Analysis designation was created.

09

My recommendation

Explain the profile before adding more sources.

The shortest useful path is to learn from the current evidence, test identity error on a bounded sample, and only then add a source that answers a named question.

NOW
Explain descriptive differences

Check group size, missing fields, and metadata quality. Define which country and activity contrasts matter for issue #5. Do not move to XGBoost yet.

NEXT
Run a bounded identity audit

Review a small stratified sample: valid single ISSN unmatched, multi-ISSN ambiguous, missing ISSN, and suspected title change. Use ISSN Portal and homepage evidence manually first.

IF NEEDED
Pilot Crossref

Use the public ISSN route only if we want DOI activity or deposit-quality evidence. Start with journal summaries, not all works.

DEFER
Scopus, Web of Science, Dimensions

Add none until we name the metric, confirm entitlement and reuse rules, and show that the source answers a question OpenAlex cannot.

What I want the group to remember

More metadata is useful only when it resolves a known gap or measures a named form of visibility.

Khanna et al.: source-reported limits and reviewer assessment

Source-reported

  • No complete estimate of all world journals.
  • Country may be server or data-center location.
  • Title and abstract text may overestimate English.
  • Language tools do not support every language.
  • Citation work is preliminary and grouped by OJS domain.
  • Metaphorical decolonization can hide or continue colonial relations.

Reviewer assessment

  • Beacon participation and the five-item rule select active transmitting installations.
  • Different analyses use different ISSN subsets and time snapshots.
  • English translation and one ANZSRC label compress multilingual, multidisciplinary journals.
  • 5.8 vs 5.4 million items and 5.7% vs 7.2% Scopus need archived-source checks.
  • All authors’ PKP link matters when reading platform-level claims.
