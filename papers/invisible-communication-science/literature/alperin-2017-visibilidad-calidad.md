# Alperin and Rozemblum (2017) — Visibility and quality in journal evaluation

- **Citation:** Alperin, J. P., & Rozemblum, C. (2017). La reinterpretación de visibilidad y calidad en las nuevas políticas de evaluación de revistas científicas. *Revista Interamericana de Bibliotecología, 40*(3), 231–241. https://doi.org/10.17533/udea.rib.v40n3a04
- **Zotero:** [Open source item in Zotero](zotero://select/library/items/H4WV769I)
- **DOI or URL:** https://doi.org/10.17533/udea.rib.v40n3a04
- **Reviewed:** 2026-07-20
- **Reading basis:** Full Spanish text, pp. 231–241, from the Universidad Nacional de La Plata institutional repository

## Why this source matters

Alperin and Rozemblum identify an evaluation-policy mechanism that can turn
database inclusion into both the definition and the reward for journal
visibility and quality. Their close reading of the 2016 Mexican and Colombian
journal-classification policies shows how broad goals such as editorial quality,
access, diffusion, and social use can become subordinate to citation ranks in
Web of Science and Scopus.

The paper therefore helps separate four constructs that are often collapsed:
index inclusion, citation impact, editorial or scientific quality, and local
social or pedagogical use. It is strongest as a contemporary interpretation of
two policy documents, not as causal evidence that the policies changed author
language, submission, or journal-survival behavior.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Analyze the development of Latin American scientific
  journals since the 1980s and examine how the 2016 Mexican and Colombian
  evaluation policies relate to the region's earlier goals of journal quality
  and visibility (abstract; pp. 232–235).
- **Conceptual framing:** A three-stage history: an emergent period (1985–1995),
  a consolidation period centered on cooperative regional systems
  (1995–2015), and a new period the authors call “internationalization”
  (2015–present). The paper contrasts a broad understanding of visibility—reach,
  access, use, and diffusion—with a narrow operationalization through citations
  captured by commercial indexes (pp. 233–235).
- **Contribution claimed:** The authors argue that the new policies retain the
  language of quality and visibility while assigning decisive rank value to
  citation-index inclusion and quartiles. They recommend returning to a
  cooperative regional model governed by the scientific community and adding
  indicators of pedagogical, governmental, and social impact (pp. 235–239).

## Methods

- **Design:** Historical and interpretive document analysis.
- **Measures or classifications:** The authors construct three periods of
  journal development, then compare the stated policy goals with the ranking
  rules that determine journal categories. Table 1 aligns the Colombian A1–C
  levels with the Mexican eight-level system and their JCR, SJR, and Google
  Scholar H5 gates (pp. 233–236).
- **Analysis:** Qualitative reading of policy language, eligibility rules, and
  category thresholds, interpreted against the development of Latindex,
  SciELO, Redalyc, national journal directories, and open access in the region
  (pp. 233–239).
- **Validation or robustness:** No systematic document-selection, coding, or
  inter-rater procedure is reported. The authors explicitly state that their
  periodization derives from their observations rather than a fixed set of
  variables and should be used as a guide rather than a definitive history
  (p. 233).

## Findings

### Earlier regional systems treated visibility and quality as multidimensional

- **Source claim:** During the consolidation period, Latin American journal
  initiatives pursued editorial quality together with visibility understood
  broadly as reach, accessibility, and diffusion, under cooperative regional
  governance (pp. 233–234).
- **Evidence:** The authors trace Latindex, SciELO, Redalyc, and national
  journal directories, noting that their criteria documented and improved
  editorial quality and that some researcher-evaluation systems recognized
  SciELO and Redalyc rather than relying only on citation impact (pp. 234–235).
- **Locator:** Section 2.2, pp. 234–235.
- **Caveat:** This is an interpretive historical account assembled from selected
  literature, not a systematic comparison of every regional platform or
  national evaluation system.

### Index inclusion becomes a gate above the Mexican policy's six dimensions

- **Source claim:** Although the Mexican policy states broad goals and evaluates
  six dimensions, inclusion in Web of Science or Scopus overrides that
  multidimensional framing by determining access to the top half of the ranking
  system (p. 236).
- **Evidence:** The policy automatically includes journals indexed in Web of
  Science or Scopus. Only journals in either database can occupy levels 1–4 of
  the eight-level system; the six dimensions then order journals within the
  indexed and non-indexed groups rather than removing the index gate (p. 236).
- **Locator:** Section 2.4 and Table 1, p. 236.
- **Caveat:** The focal paper documents the formal rule and interprets its value
  hierarchy; it does not test how evaluators applied the six dimensions in
  individual cases.

### Citation-index quartiles dominate the Colombian hierarchy

- **Source claim:** The Colombian policy operationalizes high impact primarily
  through citation-index ranks, placing journals indexed in Web of Science or
  Scopus above those classified through Google Scholar H5 (pp. 235–237).
- **Evidence:** A1 and A2 require Q1 and Q2 positions in JCR or SJR. Category B
  admits Q3 JCR/SJR or Q1 Google Scholar H5, while C admits Q4 JCR/SJR or Q2
  H5. The authors cite a contemporary assessment that only 12 Colombian
  journals were in JCR and 78 in SJR, with none in SJR Q1, implying no national
  journal could reach A1 under that snapshot (pp. 236–237).
- **Locator:** Section 2.4, Table 1, p. 236; Discussion, p. 237.
- **Caveat:** The database counts come from a cited 2017 editorial rather than
  an independently reproduced analysis in this paper, and they describe a
  historical snapshot.

### Evaluation rules create a circular relationship between visibility and quality

- **Source claim:** When citations in selectively composed commercial databases
  become the primary evidence of access, use, impact, and quality, journals
  already underrepresented in those databases receive lower value by design
  (pp. 237–238).
- **Evidence:** The authors contrast the Colombian policy's aim to make research
  accessible and useful with its assertion that citation counts show whether
  that aim was achieved. They then show that ranking advantages depend on JCR
  and SJR despite documented Latin American underrepresentation in their source
  databases (p. 237).
- **Locator:** Discussion, p. 237.
- **Caveat:** The policy-rule comparison supports a definitional and selection
  mechanism. It does not estimate the extent to which index underrepresentation
  reflects evaluation bias rather than editorial standards, journal age,
  discipline, output volume, metadata readiness, audience size, or other
  selection factors.

### Citation-centered internationalization may redirect journals away from local functions

- **Source claim:** A narrow internationalization model rewards communication
  with academic peers, especially in the Global North, while discounting the
  social and pedagogical functions of local and regional journals (pp. 237–238).
- **Evidence:** The paper notes contemporary signs of editors prioritizing
  English and cites earlier download research reporting that more than half of
  SciELO and Redalyc downloads came from students and another 20–25% from
  people unaffiliated with universities. Citation-only evaluation does not
  capture those uses (pp. 237–238).
- **Locator:** Discussion, pp. 237–238.
- **Caveat:** The focal study does not measure language switching or policy-led
  behavioral change. The download proportions come from Alperin (2015), and
  the authors infer rather than directly observe the uses made by students and
  the public.

### The paper does not reject citation or international reach outright

- **Source claim:** Internationalization is a legitimate policy objective, and
  Web of Science, Scopus, and citations can contribute to evaluation when their
  criteria improve editorial quality or when citations complement broader
  visibility indicators (pp. 235, 237).
- **Evidence:** The authors describe the governments' efforts to improve local
  science as genuine, propose a broader internationalization concept covering
  authorship and use across countries, and explicitly distinguish earlier
  complementary use of index criteria from the newer reliance on
  “international” citations as the justification (pp. 235, 237).
- **Locator:** Sections 2.3–2.4, p. 235; Discussion, p. 237.
- **Caveat:** The paper does not specify how much weight citations should retain
  or empirically compare alternative weighting schemes.

### Access and use should precede broader impact claims

- **Source claim:** Evaluation should measure access and use before claiming
  impact and should develop indicators for pedagogical, governmental, and
  social contributions within a cooperative regional model (pp. 238–239).
- **Evidence:** The authors point to emerging altmetrics as an opportunity but
  argue that new indicators alone are insufficient without regional scientific
  governance and support for locally relevant open-access publishing (pp.
  238–239).
- **Locator:** Discussion and conclusion, pp. 238–239.
- **Caveat:** This is a normative recommendation. The paper does not validate
  altmetrics or propose an operational measurement model for non-citation use.

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- The three-stage periodization is based on the authors' observations, uses no
  fixed set of variables, and highlights necessarily bounded events and trends;
  it is offered as a guide rather than a definitive history (p. 233).
- The paper focuses on journals while acknowledging that a fuller account would
  also examine researcher evaluation, the kinds of science funded, and training
  incentives (p. 238).

### Reviewer assessment

- The two 2016 policies are primary evidence for formal ranking gates, but two
  countries cannot establish a region-wide policy transition.
- The document analysis has no reported systematic search, coding protocol, or
  comparison group. Its evidence level is best treated as Level VI and overall
  Grade C: useful supporting evidence for a policy mechanism, not causal
  evidence of its behavioral or quality effects.
- The paper does not independently measure journal quality, database selection
  quality, language change, submission behavior, closures, or local impact.
  Those outcomes remain hypotheses or evidence imported from cited sources.
- Alternative explanations remain open: selective indexes may capture some
  editorial standards or international peer uptake; field, age, size, language,
  metadata, and audience composition may affect both index inclusion and
  citations; and some journals may voluntarily prioritize local communities.
- The analysis describes policy documents available in 2016–2017. It should
  not be used as evidence of current CONACYT or Colombian journal-evaluation
  rules without a fresh policy check.

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** A policy-mediated valuation mechanism in which selected
  index inclusion and citation ranks become gates for journal status while
  retaining the vocabulary of quality and visibility. It also supports keeping
  index visibility, citation impact, editorial or scientific quality, and
  social use as separate constructs.
- **Project interpretation:** Later synthesis can treat evaluation policy as an
  institutional mechanism that may amplify database coverage differences. Any
  empirical work should preserve source-specific index absence and citation
  counts as measurements, not relabel either one as journal quality.
- **Does not establish:** That non-indexed journals are high quality; that
  selective-index exclusion is caused by systemic bias; that the 2016 policies
  caused journals to publish in English or abandon local audiences; the current
  policy regimes; or the size of any causal effect on visibility, citation, or
  journal survival. It does not grant this note or later exploratory analysis
  Paper Analysis authority.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Research evaluation and incentives; index-mediated visibility;
  citation and prestige; multidimensional journal quality; regional open-access
  infrastructure; language incentives; local social and pedagogical use.
- **Agreements or tensions:** The paper adds a policy mechanism to Khanna et
  al.'s finding that selective indexes omit much OJS publishing: under some
  evaluation rules, index omission can also become a status penalty. It also
  bounds that connection because it neither measures OJS coverage nor tests
  whether index exclusion reflects policy bias, technical readiness, editorial
  selection, or journal composition.

## Open questions

- How have the Mexican and Colombian policies changed since the 2016 documents,
  and which index or citation gates remain in force?
- What longitudinal evidence links evaluation-rule changes to journal language,
  submission patterns, editorial investment, closure, or migration to
  commercial platforms?
- How much of the observed index and citation gap remains after accounting for
  field, journal age, output volume, editorial standards, metadata readiness,
  and audience size?
- Which measures can distinguish peer citation, student use, policy uptake,
  professional use, and local-language community value without treating any
  one as a universal proxy for quality?
