# Bol et al. (2018) — Matthew effect in science funding

- **Citation:** Bol, T., de Vaan, M., & van de Rijt, A. (2018). The Matthew effect in science funding. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 115*(19), 4887–4890. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1719557115
- **Zotero:** [Open source item in Zotero](zotero://select/library/items/HBZ2T6FE)
- **DOI or URL:** https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1719557115
- **Reviewed:** 2026-07-20
- **Reading basis:** Full final article, pp. 4887–4890, plus the authors' complete 29-page Supporting Information Appendix in the public OSF preprint

## Why this source matters

Bol, de Vaan, and van de Rijt use a funding cutoff to compare early-career
applicants with nearly adjacent review ranks. They find a large local effect of
winning a Dutch Veni grant on later grant participation and success, making the
paper direct evidence that an early funding decision can widen later funding
inequality rather than merely reveal pre-existing differences among applicants.

The paper also keeps two mechanisms distinct. Near-threshold winners entered the
later Vidi competition more often, while their overall probability of winning a
Vidi rose by more than the participation difference alone can establish. The
study finds no discontinuity in measured research output, but it does not
directly separate evaluator use of prior status from time or resources used to
write a stronger later proposal.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Determine whether winning an early-career Veni grant
  increases later funding success, whether the effect accumulates over time,
  and whether it operates through scientific output, evaluation, or continued
  participation in later competitions (main text, pp. 4887–4889; Supporting
  Information [SI], pp. 1–2).
- **Conceptual framing:** The Matthew effect is a cumulative-advantage process
  in which an early success supplies resources and status that increase later
  success. The authors contrast this account with delayed revelation of unequal
  ability or effort and add a participation mechanism: an early loss may reduce
  later competition through discouragement or lack of resources (p. 4887).
- **Contribution claimed:** A regression-discontinuity comparison near a hard
  funding threshold identifies a local effect of early funding; the analysis
  then tests research output and later application behavior as mechanisms and
  follows accumulated funding for eight years (pp. 4887–4890).

## Data and scope

| Element | Source-reported detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Veni grant applications and applicants near the final funding rank within an NWO division and round; later outcomes include Vidi/Vici/ERC applications or awards, accumulated grant money, bibliometric output, and 2018 professorship (pp. 4888–4889; SI pp. 4–8). |
| Data source | NWO Innovation Research Incentives Scheme application and ranking files; public NWO and ERC winner lists; Scopus publication records; and university websites for professorship status (SI pp. 4–7). |
| Sample and coverage | The master data contain 20,155 applications from 12,555 unique applicants across Veni, Vidi, and Vici, 2000–2016. The principal design uses 1,255 interviewed Veni applications within ranks −6 to +6 across 77 division-rounds from 2002–2008; 490 applications occupy the closest −2/−1 and +1/+2 groups, with 486 in Scopus-based output models (SI pp. 4–8, Table S5, Tables S6 and S8). |
| Time period | Veni treatment cohorts 2002–2008; later NWO and ERC outcomes observed through 2015/2016, bibliometric outcomes through five or more post-application years, and professorship status in January 2018 (SI pp. 4–8, 20–22). |
| Geographic or linguistic scope | Applicants to the Netherlands' NWO programme across eight research divisions. ERC matching is limited to awardees residing in the Netherlands; publication data come from Scopus. |

## Methods

- **Design:** Sharp regression-discontinuity design using final rank relative to
  the Veni funding threshold, supplemented by exact near-cutoff comparisons and
  parametric linear-probability and logistic models (p. 4888; SI pp. 8–15).
- **Measures or classifications:** Early treatment is Veni receipt. Later
  outcomes are applying for or winning Vidi/Vici awards, NWO and ERC funding
  totals, full professorship, and within-division-round standardized
  publications, log citations, and h-index before and after the Veni decision
  (SI pp. 6–8, Table S4).
- **Analysis:** The main comparison places the highest-ranked nonwinners beside
  the lowest-ranked winners. Additional models vary bandwidth and functional
  form, add division fixed effects, test post-Veni output mediation, and compare
  grant amount, field, sex, reapplications, research-direction similarity,
  interdisciplinarity, publishing exit, and migration (SI pp. 9–28).
- **Validation or robustness:** Pre-treatment publications, citations, and
  h-index show no significant cutoff differences; estimates remain positive
  across bandwidths, model forms, domains, sexes, and reapplication exclusions.
  The SI also reports a density discontinuity caused partly by gender-based
  handling of tied scores and explains why the authors believe it does not
  inflate the treatment effect (SI pp. 10–17, Figures S2–S7, Table S7).

## Findings

### Early funding substantially raises later funding success near the cutoff

- **Source claim:** Applicants who barely won a Veni were roughly 2.5 times as
  likely to win a later Vidi as applicants who barely missed the Veni (p. 4889).
- **Evidence:** The overall Vidi-winning probability was 26% for ranks +1/+2
  versus 10% for ranks −1/−2 (exact test, *p* < .001). Parametric estimates were
  similar: across specifications the Veni effect was about 12.5–18.4 percentage
  points and remained statistically significant (p. 4889; SI pp. 14–15,
  Table S7).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 1, pp. 4888–4889; SI Table S7, pp. 14–15.
- **Caveat:** This is a local threshold effect among interviewed applicants. It
  does not estimate an effect for clearly stronger or weaker applicants, people
  screened out before interview, or other funding systems.

### Lower reapplication is an important mechanism, but the paper gives no formal percentage decomposition

- **Source claim:** A substantial portion of the Vidi-success gap is associated
  with unsuccessful Veni applicants entering the Vidi competition less often
  (p. 4889).
- **Evidence:** Near-threshold Veni winners applied for Vidi at 59%, compared
  with 40% among near-threshold nonwinners, alongside the 26% versus 10% overall
  Vidi-winning probabilities (exact tests, *p* < .001). Proxy checks found no
  cutoff jump in stopping publication or employment outside the Netherlands
  (p. 4889; SI pp. 21–23, Figures S12–S13).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 1, p. 4889; SI “Leaving academia or migrating,”
  pp. 21–23.
- **Caveat:** The article does not report a causal mediation share for applicant
  behavior. The 19-percentage-point application gap and 16-percentage-point
  overall win gap cannot be converted into one mechanism percentage without an
  additional decomposition. Discouragement and lack of resources are proposed
  explanations; neither is measured directly, and the exit and migration checks
  are imperfect proxies.

### Measured research output does not explain the later funding discontinuity

- **Source claim:** The Veni award did not produce a discontinuous increase in
  measured scientific output capable of explaining the later Vidi advantage
  (p. 4889).
- **Evidence:** Publications, citations, and h-index rose with initial rank but
  did not jump at the funding cutoff before or after the Veni decision. Adding
  all three post-Veni measures changed the linear-probability estimate only from
  0.157 to 0.151, and the cumulative citation series also showed no cutoff jump
  (p. 4889; SI pp. 17–21, Tables S8–S9, Figures S10–S11).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 2, p. 4889; SI “Mediation by research output” and
  “Average growth in research output,” pp. 17–21.
- **Caveat:** A null discontinuity in Scopus publications, citations, and h-index
  is not proof of no resource benefit. These measures omit proposal quality,
  preliminary data, protected research time, team formation, unindexed outputs,
  and other achievements that evaluators may value.

### Status recognition is consistent with the evidence but is not isolated from proposal resources

- **Source claim:** Because measured output does not mediate the later advantage,
  the result is consistent with evaluators treating the earlier award itself as
  a positive status signal (pp. 4889–4890).
- **Evidence:** Veni evaluation weighted researcher and proposal quality equally,
  later competitions could see prior success, and the near-cutoff funding effect
  persisted after output adjustment. The authors interpret this as demand-side
  positive feedback (p. 4890; SI pp. 2–3, 17–18).
- **Locator:** Results and Discussion, pp. 4889–4890; SI “Evaluation of Veni
  applications,” pp. 2–3, and Table S8, p. 18.
- **Caveat:** Reviewers were not blinded to prior awards, and the design does not
  randomize the information shown to later evaluators. The authors explicitly
  retain another possibility: Veni time and resources may help winners write a
  stronger Vidi proposal even without raising bibliometric output (p. 4889).
  The study therefore identifies a bundled award effect, not a clean evaluator-
  bias or status-only effect.

### The eight-year funding gap reflects award frequency rather than larger later awards

- **Source claim:** A small initial difference at the Veni threshold widens into
  substantial later funding inequality (p. 4889).
- **Evidence:** Excluding the original €250,000 Veni, ranks +1/+2 accumulated
  about €40,000 more than ranks −1/−2 after four years and approximately
  €180,000 more after eight years (*p* < .01), more than twice the near-miss
  total. The SI reports approximately €319,000 versus €133,000. Among later ERC
  recipients, average award totals were similar (€1.596 million versus €1.564
  million, *p* = .762), so award frequency rather than size drove the gap
  (p. 4889; SI pp. 18–20, Figures S8–S9).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 3, p. 4889; SI “Accumulation of research funding,”
  pp. 18–20.
- **Caveat:** The €180,000 gap is an approximate local contrast, not the portion
  of every scientist's later funding caused by status. It equals about 40% of
  the €450,000 difference between the highest- and lowest-scoring interviewees,
  but that second comparison is descriptive rather than randomized.

### The award is associated with later academic rank as well as funding

- **Source claim:** Winning the early grant increased the long-term probability
  of becoming a full professor (pp. 4889–4890).
- **Evidence:** Professorship in 2018 was 28% for ranks +1/+2 versus 19% for ranks
  −1/−2, a reported 47% relative increase (*p* < .05) 10–16 years after the
  Veni competition (Figure 4, pp. 4889–4890).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 4, pp. 4889–4890.
- **Caveat:** Professorship was reconstructed from public websites, five cases
  were excluded as ambiguous, and the paper does not identify which subsequent
  funding, institutional, mobility, or hiring process produced the difference
  (SI p. 6).

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- The design identifies applicants near the Veni cutoff and does not establish
  effects for the full applicant distribution; the SI also notes that sample
  sizes prevent finer field disaggregation (SI pp. 8, 16–17).
- The forcing variable is discrete and shows a small density change at the
  cutoff, consistent with committees sometimes using gender to decide among
  tied applicants. A standard McCrary density test could not be used; the
  authors argue that placing equal-quality tied applicants on both sides should
  not inflate the award effect (SI pp. 12–14).
- Leaving academia and migration are assessed with publication and current-
  affiliation proxies rather than complete career histories (SI pp. 21–23).
- Raw NWO data cannot be publicly shared for legal reasons. The OSF package
  supplies code and condensed data for two central figures, not full independent
  reproduction of every analysis (OSF project documentation).

### Reviewer assessment

- The regression discontinuity provides strong Level III quasi-experimental
  evidence for the local bundled effect of receiving a Veni grant and an overall
  Grade A for that claim. It does not support the same grade for a universal
  Matthew effect, a status-only mechanism, or current funding practice.
- “Demand side” is partly interpretive. The design observes later applications,
  awards, and outputs, but not evaluators' counterfactual scores with the prior
  award hidden. Status recognition, better later proposals, protected time, and
  unmeasured resources remain entangled.
- “Supply side” should be read as applicant participation, not supply of funding.
  It is a behavioral outcome at the applicant level and remains distinct from
  evaluator decisions and resource-enabled achievement.
- The analytic unit is an application near a threshold, while some people apply
  repeatedly. Robustness exclusions preserve the discontinuity, but application-
  level estimates should not be relabeled as population-wide career effects.
- The study concerns the Dutch Veni system in 2002–2008, when researcher and
  proposal quality each received 50% of the score and applicants did not know
  their final rank. Generalization requires similar threshold rules, visibility
  of prior awards, repeated eligibility, and limited feedback.
- No conflict of interest was declared. No retraction or correction signal was
  identified in the DOI, Crossref, NCBI, or Zotero records checked for this
  review.

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** A threshold-local causal effect of early funding on later
  funding participation and success; a widening eight-year funding gap; and the
  need to model applicant reapplication separately from evaluator decisions and
  measured research output.
- **Project interpretation:** Uneven scholarly recognition can be amplified by a
  feedback loop in which an early gate changes both who returns to compete and
  how later applications fare. Later synthesis should therefore distinguish
  award receipt, resources, evaluator recognition, applicant participation,
  research output, later funding, and academic rank as separate constructs and
  outcomes.
- **Does not establish:** That reviewers consciously favor prestige; that the
  €180,000 gap is wholly due to status; that research resources have no effect;
  that rejected applicants lack merit; that the effect applies far from the
  threshold or outside comparable funding systems; or that funding inequality
  explains journal discoverability, citation circulation, or non-academic use.
  It does not grant this note or the corpus Paper Analysis authority.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Matthew effect; cumulative funding advantage; grant peer review;
  status cues; resource advantage; applicant participation; discouragement;
  threshold decisions; academic careers; meritocracy.
- **Agreements or tensions:** The source supplies a direct positive-feedback
  mechanism for later recognition while preserving a behavioral counter-
  explanation: near-miss applicants partly remove themselves from subsequent
  competition. It also limits a pure gatekeeper account because measurable
  output does not explain the discontinuity, yet status and proposal-resource
  mechanisms remain observationally bundled.

## Open questions

- What proportion of the later Vidi-winning gap is causally mediated by
  reapplication, and how sensitive is that share to the order and model used for
  decomposition?
- Would hiding prior award information from later reviewers reduce the funding
  discontinuity while holding proposal content constant?
- Which resource channels—protected time, staff, pilot data, networks, or grant-
  writing support—change later proposal quality without appearing in
  publications, citations, or h-index?
- Does the 2026 multi-funder replication preserve the participation mechanism,
  and which institutional features moderate it outside the Dutch Veni system?
- Do similar feedback processes operate for citation, publication acceptance,
  journal index admission, or professional and policy use, or is the mechanism
  specific to repeated individual grant competitions?
