# Bosman et al. (2021) — OA Diamond Journals Study

- **Citation:** Bosman, J., Frantsvåg, J. E., Kramer, B., Langlais, P.-C., & Proudman, V. (2021). *OA Diamond Journals Study. Part 1: Findings*. Zenodo. https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.4558704
- **Zotero:** [Open source item in Zotero](zotero://select/library/items/EL9E6RJT)
- **DOI or URL:** https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.4558704
- **Reviewed:** 2026-07-20
- **Reading basis:** Full text, pp. 1–203

## Why this source matters

Bosman et al. provide unusually broad evidence about journals that charge neither
readers nor authors. The report connects partial integration into indexes and
technical standards with small scale, limited paid capacity, informal governance,
volunteer dependence, and the cost of shared infrastructure. It is therefore useful
for distinguishing technical nonintegration from political-economic constraint,
while also showing that autonomy, local orientation, and noncommercial governance
are not simply deficits.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Describe the global OA diamond landscape, identify its
  funding models and technical and organisational challenges, and assess the
  potential for collaboration and shared services (pp. 6–8).
- **Conceptual framing:** OA diamond publishing is treated as a diverse,
  community-driven ecosystem whose journals interact with owners, volunteers,
  platforms, infrastructures, indexers, funders, and research institutions. The
  report uses Plan S technical requirements as one integration benchmark and later
  describes a developing culture of “OA commons publishing” (pp. 12, 49–52,
  103–105).
- **Contribution claimed:** The study makes a previously obscured publishing
  “archipelago” visible, documents both its scientific strengths and operational
  constraints, and argues that coordinated, shared, and sustained support could
  preserve its diversity while improving technical integration (pp. 6–8, 103–105).

## Data and scope

| Element | Source-reported detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | OA diamond journal; some qualitative evidence concerns hosting platforms and infrastructures (pp. 12–18). |
| Data source | DOAJ, ROAD, and Crawford's Gold Open Access databases; a 94-question online survey; 7,019 free-text submissions; a 160-reference literature search; three journal focus groups; and hosting-platform interviews (pp. 13–18). |
| Sample and coverage | 1,619 valid journal responses after cleaning approximately 1,900 identifiable and 2,605 raw survey events; 1,136 respondents said their journal was in DOAJ, 431 said it was not, and 52 did not know. The qualitative work included three focus groups with 11 journals and 10 platform interviews (pp. 7, 13, 18). |
| Time period | Survey preparation and collection ran from June to September 2020. Database snapshots range from January 2020 to February 2021; the report is dated March 2021 (pp. 2, 13, 16–17). |

## Methods

- **Design:** Descriptive mixed-methods landscape study combining database
  analysis, a nonprobability survey, computational and qualitative analysis of
  free text, and semi-structured focus groups and interviews (pp. 13–18).
- **Measures or classifications:** Survey domains included governance,
  authorship, content and metadata, editorial quality, technical framework,
  funding, dissemination, and challenges. DOAJ data replaced 21 questions for
  respondents already listed there. Comparisons cover fee model, journal and
  publisher size, region, discipline, language, ownership, host type, standards,
  costs, staffing, and volunteer reliance (pp. 13, 16–17, 52).
- **Analysis:** The report presents counts, percentages, subgroup comparisons,
  cost-per-article estimates, and a correspondence analysis. Free-text analysis
  combined semantic mapping and clustering with manual review of longer answers
  and manual annotation of small HDBSCAN clusters (pp. 14–16, 103–104).
- **Validation or robustness:** Responses were deduplicated and fake answers
  removed. Database sources were compared and dated, the report discusses their
  different inclusion rules, and qualitative clusters were manually interpreted
  rather than treated as valid statistics. No probability weighting, causal
  identification, or formal external peer review is reported (pp. 13–18).

## Findings

### Integration gaps cluster in technical standards rather than editorial review

- **Source claim:** OA diamond journals were not fully integrated into the
  technical standards represented by Plan S, although peer review was widespread
  and reported adherence to publication-practice guidelines was high.
- **Evidence:** Among DOAJ OA diamond journals, 55.3% used article PIDs, 19.1%
  reported a recognised permanent-preservation solution, and 43.6% embedded a
  machine-readable license; all but one DOAJ journal reported a qualifying peer
  review process. Only 4.3% met all six assessed requirements, while 37% met more
  than half (pp. 53–60, 73–74; Executive Summary, pp. 7–8).
- **Locator:** Sections 2.1–2.2 and 2.5, pp. 53–74.
- **Caveat:** Plan S is a funder-defined benchmark rather than a complete measure
  of scientific quality or social value. Some reported services could not be
  classified, and “unknown” responses were common on technical questions (pp.
  58–60, 67).

### Platforms and pooled competence are associated with greater technical integration

- **Source claim:** Hosting platforms can spread the fixed costs and expertise
  needed for identifiers, structured formats, preservation, workflow management,
  and standards across many journals.
- **Evidence:** Sixty percent of survey respondents used OJS, but independent OJS
  journals were predominantly PDF-only while journals on several shared platforms
  more often supplied structured text. International platforms and commercial
  hosts reported DOI use for 75% and 82% of hosted journals respectively, and
  platform interviews indicated greater readiness for Plan S requirements than
  individual-journal accounts (pp. 93–98).
- **Locator:** Sections 3.3.1–3.3.2, pp. 93–98; Section 3.4, pp. 103–105.
- **Caveat:** These are cross-sectional associations. Better-resourced or more
  technically mature journals may be more likely to join platforms, so the report
  does not isolate a platform effect.

### Resource capacity shapes whether journals can meet integration requirements

- **Source claim:** Journal size, budget, staffing, and access to professional
  competence help explain differences in standards compliance and index coverage.
- **Evidence:** Smaller, university-based, HSS, and OA diamond journals met fewer
  Plan S requirements than larger or professionally published journals. Respondents
  repeatedly identified DOI fees, anti-plagiarism tools, JATS-XML conversion,
  preservation, training, and index applications as costs. Reported Scopus coverage
  rose from about one-quarter of journals with annual costs below $/€1,000 to 68%
  of journals above $/€50,000 (pp. 73–74, 98–102).
- **Locator:** Section 2.5, pp. 73–74; Section 3.3.3, pp. 98–102.
- **Caveat:** Budget, size, age, host type, field, and professionalisation covary.
  The study does not show which factor independently causes index inclusion.

### Informal governance preserves autonomy but can diffuse responsibility

- **Source claim:** Community ownership supports editorial autonomy and diverse
  formats, but weak formalisation can make responsibility and succession uncertain.
- **Evidence:** Universities owned 42% of surveyed journals. Only 51% of respondents
  reported a legal ownership document; 23% reported none and 26% did not know.
  Documented ownership increased with staff and budget, and the report connects
  missing documentation with uncertainty about who can address structural
  problems (pp. 79–83). Focus-group participants also valued independence and the
  ability to use unusual formats or decision structures (p. 83).
- **Locator:** Section 3.1, pp. 79–84.
- **Caveat:** Legal formalisation is not equivalent to good governance. Country
  differences may reflect legal or organisational cultures, and several national
  subsamples were too small for firm conclusions (p. 81).

### Volunteer and institutional support keep costs low while hiding fragility

- **Source claim:** OA diamond publishing is sustained mainly through voluntary
  labour and institutional or government support, which enables low monetary
  costs but creates succession, accounting, and continuity risks.
- **Evidence:** Sixty percent of respondents used volunteers; among those reporting
  a reliance level, 86% described it as medium or high. More than 60% reported total
  annual costs below $/€10,000, and over 80% reported paid staffing of no more than
  2 FTE. Forty-three percent said they broke even, 25% reported a loss, and 31% did
  not know their financial status. Research-performing organisations, governments,
  in-kind institutional support, and voluntary labour were the dominant support
  sources and mechanisms (pp. 110–121).
- **Locator:** Sections 4.1.1–4.1.5, pp. 110–121; Section 4.3, pp. 124–126.
- **Caveat:** Reported costs may omit voluntary labour and institutional in-kind
  contributions, use broad cost brackets, and reflect different local price levels.
  Low expenditure can indicate efficiency, underfunding, or unrecorded support;
  the study cannot distinguish them consistently (pp. 111–113, 123–125).

### Indexation is both an administrative burden and a contested recognition system

- **Source claim:** Indexation was the most prominent technical challenge, with
  respondents describing complex procedures, shifting requirements, prestige
  thresholds, language demands, and a feedback loop between small scale and
  noninclusion.
- **Evidence:** Indexation ranked highest among technical challenges. Respondents
  linked applications to the cost of identifiers, plagiarism checks, structured
  files, staff time, and multiple incompatible criteria; some also described English
  requirements and reliance on Scopus or JCR in assessment as unequal (pp.
  101–103).
- **Locator:** Section 3.3.3.4, pp. 101–103.
- **Caveat:** The evidence here is mainly self-report and selected qualitative
  testimony. The study does not audit individual rejection decisions or compare
  excluded and included journals on identical editorial and technical criteria.
  Editors may also underreport systems that index them, so the landscape chapter
  treats survey coverage figures as lower bounds (p. 44).

### Partial integration is not synonymous with failure or unwanted isolation

- **Source claim:** OA diamond journals often serve national author communities,
  publish multilingual work, and value autonomy while reaching audiences beyond
  their home countries; shared support should retain this bibliodiversity rather
  than force one publishing model.
- **Evidence:** National authorship was common, but only 5–10% of journals across
  regions drew most authors from their owning institution. Many journals reported
  international readership, and DOAJ OA diamond journals were more often
  multilingual than APC-based journals (38% versus 14%). Seventy-three percent of
  respondents had no intention of moving away from OA diamond publishing (pp.
  40–44, 122–124).
- **Locator:** Sections 1.4–1.5, pp. 40–45; Sections 4.1.6–4.2, pp. 122–124.
- **Caveat:** Authorship, readership, accepted-language, and business-model
  intentions are not measures of citation circulation, prestige, policy uptake, or
  long-term survival.

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- The survey's dissemination routes, funder's scope, and consortium composition
  created geographic bias, and respondents may have participated either to show
  viability or to emphasise need (pp. 13–14).
- The 1,619-journal sample is small relative to the estimated population and is
  described as most representative of Europe and the Americas (p. 78).
- Free-text participation was incomplete, brief answers were often unusable, and
  long answers could be overrepresented. Clusters were exploratory and required
  qualitative interpretation (pp. 14–16).
- DOAJ depends on journal applications and changing self-reported metadata; ROAD
  lacks fee data and strict vetting; database snapshots and selection rules differ
  (pp. 16–17).
- Cost-per-article estimates use broad brackets, cover only 965 journals, may omit
  in-kind and volunteer costs, and do not adjust for local price levels (pp. 111–113).

### Reviewer assessment

- This is a commissioned institutional report rather than a peer-reviewed journal
  article. cOAlition S and Science Europe funded or supported it, and consortium
  members include OA advocacy, directory, platform, and infrastructure
  organisations. These relationships are disclosed on pp. 4–6 but create an
  institutional and intellectual interest in the OA diamond model.
- The report combines multiple datasets and denominators. For example, DOI and
  preservation percentages differ between DOAJ, survey, journal-level, and
  article-level analyses; downstream reuse should retain the named source and
  denominator rather than quote one headline rate (pp. 57–60, 73–74, 96–97).
- Associations between platforms, resources, standards, and indexing do not
  establish direction of causation. Journal age, field, size, host selection,
  editorial practice, and metadata readiness remain competing explanations.
- Respondent accounts of index bias and a “vicious circle” establish perceived
  mechanisms and burdens, not verified discrimination by an index. Conversely,
  technical criteria can themselves embed resource and language inequalities;
  treating them as neutral would also exceed the evidence.
- “Integration” spans identifiers, metadata, preservation, hosting, index inclusion,
  and standards compliance. The report does not test whether any one of these
  produces more citations, prestige, policy use, or epistemic recognition.

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** A multi-level explanation of OA diamond nonintegration in
  which journal resources and governance, platform capacity, technical standards,
  and index requirements interact. It supports treating missing identifiers,
  structured metadata, preservation, and administrative capacity as mechanisms
  that can make existing scholarship harder to discover.
- **Project interpretation:** Later synthesis can use this source to connect the
  Khanna-derived infrastructure path with political economy: standards are not
  merely metadata fields when journals must purchase or collectively supply the
  labour, tools, and expertise needed to meet them. At the same time, autonomy,
  local service, and multilingual publishing should remain separate from technical
  deficit or lack of ambition.
- **Does not establish:** A causal effect of OA diamond ownership or platform use
  on visibility; that index exclusion is always discriminatory; that included
  journals meet the same substantive quality; current OA diamond conditions; or
  any effect on citations, evaluation, policy uptake, or knowledge recognition.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Infrastructure and interoperability; resource capacity; community
  governance; scholarly communication as a commons; index and standard setting;
  volunteer labour; preservation; multilingual and locally oriented publishing;
  political economy of noncommercial OA.
- **Agreements or tensions:** The report gives direct descriptive support to
  Khanna et al.'s infrastructure and OA diamond paths, but narrows their use: weak
  integration can arise from resource scarcity, administrative readiness, journal
  size, or deliberate community orientation as well as exclusionary recognition
  systems. It also shows that quality-control practices and technical compliance
  must not be collapsed.

## Open questions

- After matching on journal age, field, size, metadata completeness, and editorial
  practice, how much of index noncoverage remains associated with language,
  geography, ownership, or business model?
- Do shared platforms improve standards and discoverability, or mostly select
  journals that were already capable of meeting entry requirements?
- Which technical requirements have the strongest relationship with article-level
  discovery, and which function mainly as administrative admission criteria?
- How should hidden volunteer and in-kind labour be valued without treating every
  community contribution as exploitation or every low-cost model as sustainable?
- Have identifiers, preservation, structured full text, index inclusion, and
  financial stability changed since the 2020 survey?
