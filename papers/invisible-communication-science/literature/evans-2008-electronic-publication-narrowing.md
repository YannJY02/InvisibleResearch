## Why this source matters

Evans tests whether putting deeper and broader journal archives online changes
which prior scholarship authors cite. The study links online availability to
younger references, fewer distinct cited articles and journals, and, in several
models, greater citation concentration. It belongs in this corpus as an
attention-allocation account within already indexed literature, but its search
and hyperlink mechanism is an interpretation of observational citation patterns
rather than a directly observed user-behaviour effect.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Test whether the depth and breadth of journal issues
  available online predict the age, number, and concentration of references
  made by later articles, relative to the same journals or subfields at other
  points in time (pp. 395–398).
- **Conceptual framing:** Print browsing exposes readers to historically and
  topically adjacent work, whereas electronic search and citation hyperlinks
  let readers select relevant work and encounter others' prior choices more
  directly. Evans proposes that this efficiency can reinforce prevailing
  opinion through preferential attachment and reduce serendipitous contact with
  marginally related work (pp. 395, 398).
- **Contribution claimed:** As journal archives came online, authors cited more
  recent work, cited fewer distinct articles and journals, and concentrated
  citations on fewer sources. Evans interprets these associations as evidence
  that online search and hyperlinking narrow the scholarly past and present on
  which new work builds (pp. 395–399).

## Data and scope

| Element | Source-reported detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Outcomes are aggregated within citing journal-year and subfield-year panels; cited articles and journals are the units over which reference age, distinct counts, and concentration are calculated (pp. 395–398). |
| Data source | Thomson Scientific's Science, Social Science, and Arts and Humanities Citation Indexes, linked by ISSN to Information Today, Inc.'s *Fulltext Sources Online* records of commercial and free journal availability (pp. 395–396). |
| Sample and coverage | 26,002,796 articles in journals that came online and a distinct 8,090,813 articles that cited them. The Citation Index covered about 6,000 highly cited journals and assigned most journals to one to three of roughly 300 content codes (pp. 395–396). |
| Time period | Citation records run from 1945–2005 and online-availability records begin in 1998. The main 20-year-window models use 1965–2005; expanding-window checks use 1975–2005 so that 1- to 30-year windows are comparable (pp. 395–398). |
| Geographic or linguistic scope | Not reported. The analysis is not disaggregated by country, region, language, institution, author, or scholarly community. |

## Methods

- **Design:** Longitudinal observational panel analysis comparing citation
  patterns within journals and subfields as their commercial and free online
  backfiles changed (pp. 395–398).
- **Measures or classifications:** Online availability is measured as years of
  back issues available in commercial archives or freely on journal websites.
  Outcomes are mean reference age, the distinct numbers of cited articles and
  journals, and Herfindahl–Hirschman concentration among cited articles and
  journals, usually within a 20-year reference window (pp. 395–398).
- **Analysis:** Ordinary least squares models estimate mean citation age and
  Herfindahl–Hirschman concentration. Maximum-likelihood negative-binomial
  models estimate distinct citation counts. Models include citation year,
  average article pages, and average reference-list length; citation-age models
  also control for the weighted age of title words to separate archive effects
  from newer research topics (pp. 396–398).
- **Validation or robustness:** Commercial and free availability are modeled
  together; the author reports similar results after accounting for journals'
  presence in multiple commercial archives. The outcomes are also recalculated
  with expanding 1- to 30-year windows, with 95% confidence intervals shown in
  Figure 2D–E (pp. 397–398). Detailed Methods and Tables S1–S4 are in Supporting
  Online Material that was not retrieved in this review, so their coefficients
  and specifications were not independently assessed.

## Findings

### Deeper online backfiles are associated with younger references

- **Source claim:** Increasing the years of journal issues available online is
  associated with references to more recent articles (pp. 396–397).
- **Evidence:** For a model journal at the sample mean of 5.6 years per
  reference, adding 15 years of both commercial and free online availability
  predicts a fall to just under 4.5 years. The modeled change is about 0.088
  fewer years of reference age for each additional year of online availability;
  models within subfields have the same direction but wider confidence
  intervals (Figure 2A, pp. 396–397).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 2A, pp. 396–397.
- **Caveat:** Online backfile depth is an availability measure, not an observed
  search query, hyperlink path, or reading event. The estimate is conditional
  on the model and does not isolate a user-interface mechanism.

### More online availability predicts fewer distinct cited sources

- **Source claim:** Online provision suppresses the growth in distinct articles
  and journals cited relative to the counterfactual modeled for the same
  journals and subfields (p. 397).
- **Evidence:** For model units at sample-average citation levels, five
  additional years of both commercial and free availability predict a decline
  from about 600 to 200 distinct articles cited within a journal, from 25,000 to
  15,000 articles cited within a subfield, and from 19 to 16 journals cited
  within a subfield. One additional year of free online availability is
  associated with a 14% decline in distinct articles cited (Figure 2B, p. 397).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 2B, p. 397.
- **Caveat:** These are model-based examples, not before-and-after raw counts
  for a single journal. The total number of distinct sources cited continued to
  grow over calendar time even while online availability had a negative
  conditional association.

### Concentration effects vary by access channel and aggregation level

- **Source claim:** Online availability generally concentrates citations on
  fewer articles and journals, but the sign and magnitude are not uniform
  across commercial/free access and journal/subfield outcomes (p. 397).
- **Evidence:** Ten additional years of commercial access predict an increase
  in within-journal article concentration from the sample mean of 0.088 to
  0.105, nearly 20%. Free access has a slight negative association with
  article concentration within journals, a marginal positive association with
  article concentration within subfields, and a stronger positive association
  with concentration on central journals within subfields. Commercial access
  is consistently positive across the reported concentration models (Figure
  2C, p. 397).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 2C, p. 397.
- **Caveat:** The headline narrowing claim compresses heterogeneous model
  results. A single statement that all online access raises every form of
  concentration would exceed the evidence.

### The distinct-source association is strongest for recent citation windows

- **Source claim:** Online availability narrows recent reference selection more
  strongly than selection across longer historical windows (pp. 397–398).
- **Evidence:** With windows expanded from 1 to 30 years, one additional year
  of online availability corresponds to a 9% drop in articles cited from the
  most recent year and about a 7% drop in articles cited across 20- and 30-year
  windows. The same direction appears for articles and journals within
  subfields. Article concentration within subfields rises most—about 1.5% per
  year of online availability—when only the most recent year's articles are
  considered (Figure 2D–E, pp. 397–398).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 2D–E, pp. 397–398.
- **Caveat:** Concentration estimates across the expanding windows are less
  consistent than the distinct-count estimates, which the author acknowledges.

### Search, hyperlinks, and accelerated consensus remain proposed mechanisms

- **Source claim:** Search lets authors avoid older or marginally relevant work,
  while hyperlinks reveal prevailing choices and can amplify them through
  preferential attachment, hastening consensus (p. 398).
- **Evidence:** The observed availability coefficients point in the direction
  predicted by this account, and prior library-use studies are cited to show a
  shift from browsing toward search and hyperlink following (pp. 395, 398).
- **Locator:** Framing and discussion, pp. 395 and 398.
- **Caveat:** The study does not observe individual search results, ranking,
  clicks, reading, reference selection, or consensus formation. The behavioural
  mechanism is therefore an interpretation consistent with the outcomes, not a
  tested mediator.

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- References placed in online Supporting Material, including by *Science*, are
  not indexed by the Citation Index and could be older or more diverse than the
  references observed in the main articles (p. 398).
- The journal-only design excludes blogs, wikis, and alternative online
  publishing or review outlets, which may link to very recent but also more
  diverse material (p. 398).
- Concentration effects across different citation-window lengths are not fully
  consistent, unlike the distinct-source results (pp. 397–398).

### Reviewer assessment

- The study identifies associations between archive availability and citation
  outcomes, not a causal effect of online search or hyperlink use. Online
  availability changes with calendar time and may coincide with unmeasured
  changes in database coverage, journal composition, publication volume,
  search interfaces, or scholarly practice.
- The Citation Index's roughly 6,000 highly cited journals define the observed
  system. The analysis cannot show whether online search narrows or broadens
  visibility for journals and communities excluded from those indexes.
- Journal-year and subfield-year averages cannot establish which authors,
  institutions, countries, languages, or communities gain or lose attention.
  The outcomes are reference-selection distributions, not direct measures of
  discoverability, prestige, evaluation, policy use, or professional uptake.
- The article is a strong observational panel for the association it estimates
  (discipline-relative evidence level IV), but its proposed behavioural
  mechanism and normative claim about harmful narrowing require different
  evidence. The unavailable Supporting Online Material also limits review of
  specification details and coefficient tables.

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** Within selective citation indexes, deeper and broader
  online journal availability is associated with younger, less varied, and in
  several models more concentrated reference selection. It also supplies a
  plausible attention-allocation mechanism in which efficient search and
  visible prior choices reinforce already prominent work.
- **Project interpretation:** The project can treat online interfaces as a
  mechanism that may redistribute attention after a source is already present
  in the searchable corpus. This mechanism is analytically distinct from index
  admission, metadata readiness, prestige evaluation, and group-level
  recognition, and should be tested at its own outcome and unit of analysis.
- **Does not establish:** A causal search or hyperlink effect; unequal index
  admission; disadvantage by region, language, institution, journal, author,
  or scholarly community; that concentration is necessarily harmful; that the
  1998–2005 associations describe current discovery platforms; or that
  Larivière et al.'s aggregate dispersion result is false. It does not grant
  this note or the corpus Paper Analysis authority.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Electronic search and browsing; reference age; citation breadth
  and concentration; preferential attachment; consensus formation; interface
  effects; unit-of-analysis and denominator boundaries; competing explanations
  for uneven visibility.
- **Agreements or tensions:** Evans and Larivière et al. address related
  citation distributions but do not estimate the same outcome. Evans has more
  leverage for a conditional association with online-availability exposure;
  Larivière et al. have broader descriptive leverage for the long-run direction
  of citation dispersion across publication cohorts. Neither design identifies
  a causal behavioural mechanism, and both remain bounded by selective-index
  coverage.

## Open questions

- Does the association remain after separating online availability from search
  ranking, literature growth, database expansion, and changes in reference-list
  norms?
- Do modern discovery systems broaden or narrow cross-field recombination when
  actual queries, result rankings, clicks, and downstream citations are linked?
- Can a decomposition show whether narrower reference lists within citing
  articles coexist with broader citation participation across annual paper
  cohorts, as the Evans–Larivière boundary predicts?
- Which regions, languages, institutions, journals, authors, and communities
  gain or lose attention after entering a searchable index?
- Do the unavailable Supporting Online Material specifications and Tables
  S1–S4 change the interpretation of any headline coefficient?
