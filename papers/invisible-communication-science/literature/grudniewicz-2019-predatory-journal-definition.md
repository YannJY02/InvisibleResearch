# Grudniewicz et al. (2019) — A consensus boundary for predatory journals

- **Citation:** Grudniewicz, A., Moher, D., Cobey, K. D., et al. (2019).
  Predatory journals: No definition, no defence. *Nature, 576*(7786),
  210–212. https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-019-03759-y
- **Zotero:** [Open source item in Zotero](zotero://select/library/items/85WZFKJG)
- **DOI or URL:** https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-019-03759-y
- **Reviewed:** 2026-07-20
- **Reading basis:** Full text, publisher PDF pp. 210–212

## Why this source matters

Grudniewicz et al. report a 43-participant consensus process intended to make
the label “predatory” more coherent and usable. The resulting definition
locates the boundary in conduct and publishing practices rather than in open
access (OA), article processing charges (APCs), list membership, or low
resources alone. For this project, the Comment supplies a defensible conceptual
starting point for journal selection while also showing why the boundary is not
yet an empirical classifier or evidence of a downstream visibility effect.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Develop an agreed definition of predatory journals and
  publishers that can support later prevalence research, comparison, and
  interventions (opening discussion and “Road to consensus,” pp. 210–211).
- **Conceptual framing:** The authors frame predatory publishing as conduct that
  puts an entity's self-interest ahead of scholarship and manifests through
  misleading representation, departure from editorial or publication norms,
  opacity, or aggressive indiscriminate solicitation. They distinguish these
  practices from journal quality, adequacy of peer review, assessed intent to
  deceive, resource constraints, and OA as a business or access model (“The
  definition,” “Details matter,” and “Criteria we left out,” pp. 211–212).
- **Contribution claimed:** A multi-stakeholder consensus definition and an
  account of how it was reached, offered as a reference point for future
  measurement and intervention rather than as a validated journal-classification
  instrument (“The definition,” “Road to consensus,” and “Next steps,”
  pp. 211–212).

## Data and scope

| Element | Source-reported detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Participants' judgments about the definition, terminology, and candidate characteristics of predatory journals and publishers; no journals were sampled or classified (“Road to consensus,” p. 211). |
| Data source | An 18-question, 28-sub-question modified Delphi survey, 12 hours of discussion, and subsequent feedback and revision (“Road to consensus,” p. 211). |
| Sample and coverage | 43 participants from 10 countries representing publishing societies, funders, researchers, policymakers, academic institutions, libraries, and patient partners (“Road to consensus,” p. 211). Recruitment, country counts, and participant responses are not reported in the Comment. |
| Time period | Two-day summit in Ottawa, Canada, in April 2019 (“Road to consensus,” p. 211). |
| Geographic or linguistic scope | Participants came from 10 countries. The discussion focused on biomedical sciences; the authors propose broader applicability. Meeting language and non-biomedical validation are not reported (“Road to consensus,” p. 211). |

## Methods

- **Design:** Expert and stakeholder consensus exercise reported as a *Nature*
  Comment (“Road to consensus,” p. 211).
- **Measures or classifications:** A three-round modified Delphi process covered
  18 questions and 28 sub-questions. The final boundary combines an overarching
  self-interest-at-scholarship's-expense condition with one or more observable
  practice categories: misleading representation, deviation from best
  editorial or publication practices, lack of transparency, and aggressive
  indiscriminate solicitation (“The definition” and “Road to consensus,”
  p. 211).
- **Analysis:** Participants reviewed structured questions, discussed them for
  12 hours, and completed two further feedback and revision rounds. The Comment
  narrates the difficult choices around terminology and excluded criteria but
  does not report item-level votes or a quantitative consensus analysis (“Road
  to consensus,” p. 211).
- **Validation or robustness:** Stakeholder and country diversity plus iterative
  feedback broaden the deliberative base. No preregistered protocol, recruitment
  frame, attrition record, consensus threshold, item-level response data,
  inter-rater agreement, or validation against known journals is reported
  (“Road to consensus” and “Next steps,” pp. 211–212).

## Findings

### The consensus boundary concerns practices, not an access model

- **Source claim:** Predatory journals and publishers should be distinguished by
  conduct that subordinates scholarship to self-interest and by specified
  misleading, substandard, opaque, or indiscriminately solicitous practices
  (“The definition” and “Details matter,” pp. 211–212).
- **Evidence:** The definition reached through the modified Delphi process names
  four practice categories. The ensuing explanation gives examples such as
  false indexing or editorial-board claims, absent policies or licensing
  information, undisclosed fees or unverifiable editors, and repeated or
  out-of-scope invitations (“The definition,” “Road to consensus,” and “Details
  matter,” pp. 211–212).
- **Locator:** “The definition,” “Road to consensus,” and “Details matter,”
  pp. 211–212.
- **Caveat:** The definition contains broad and partly disjunctive categories;
  the Comment does not operationalize a score, threshold, evidentiary standard,
  or decision procedure for admitting or excluding a journal.

### Deceptive representation and integrity practice remain distinct

- **Source claim:** False or misleading claims are one boundary component,
  whereas departures from best editorial or publication practices and lack of
  transparency are separate components (“Details matter,” pp. 211–212).
- **Evidence:** The authors separate misrepresentation of indexes, impact,
  addresses, editorial boards, or peer-review rigour from practices such as
  missing retraction or licensing policies and from absent disclosure of fees,
  decision procedures, or editorial identities (“Details matter,” pp. 211–212).
- **Locator:** “Details matter,” pp. 211–212.
- **Caveat:** An observed integrity deficiency is not automatically proof of
  deceptive intent. The authors deliberately omit intent to deceive because it
  is difficult to assess (“Criteria we left out,” p. 212).

### Low resources, low quality, and inadequate peer review are not sufficient selectors

- **Source claim:** A poorly resourced journal can resemble a predatory journal,
  and journal quality, adequacy of peer review, and intent to deceive are too
  difficult or subjective to include in the consensus definition (“Details
  matter” and “Criteria we left out,” p. 212).
- **Evidence:** The Comment notes that a legitimate resource-constrained journal
  might have an unprofessional website, that new journals face waiting periods
  before joining some organizations, and that many legitimate journals do not
  expose enough peer-review information for assessment (“Details matter” and
  “Criteria we left out,” pp. 211–212).
- **Locator:** “Details matter,” pp. 211–212; “Criteria we left out,” p. 212.
- **Caveat:** This protects resource constraint from being treated as misconduct,
  but it also leaves the overarching self-interest condition and the threshold
  for combined warning signs insufficiently operationalized.

### Index or list admission does not determine the integrity boundary

- **Source claim:** Membership in an ethics organization, curated index, or
  journal directory does not guarantee quality, and existing legitimate and
  predatory lists disagree (“Road to consensus,” p. 211).
- **Evidence:** The “No list to rule them all” figure shows overlapping and
  conflicting classifications among Beall's list, two Cabells lists, and DOAJ;
  the text also notes that problematic journals can enter lists and that new
  journals can be ineligible during waiting periods (“No list to rule them all”
  and “Road to consensus,” p. 211).
- **Locator:** “No list to rule them all” figure and “Road to consensus,” p. 211.
- **Caveat:** The figure is adapted from prior research. This Comment neither
  audits index-admission decisions nor estimates false-positive or
  false-negative rates, so it cannot establish index fairness.

### Consensus establishes wording, not prevalence or visibility outcomes

- **Source claim:** The definition is intended to enable later tracking of the
  prevalence and influence of predatory publishing and evaluation of
  interventions (“The definition” and “Next steps,” pp. 211–212).
- **Evidence:** The authors describe future work: a multilingual portal, an
  international observatory, discipline- and geography-specific publication
  counts, stakeholder resources, and a tested digital assessment tool (“Next
  steps,” p. 212).
- **Locator:** “The definition,” p. 211; “Next steps,” p. 212.
- **Caveat:** The opening examples and estimates come from cited studies, not
  from this consensus exercise. The Comment provides no journal sample,
  prevalence denominator, index-admission analysis, or measured outcome for
  discoverability, readership, citation, submission, hiring, funding, policy,
  professional use, or other forms of scholarly visibility.

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- The summit focused on biomedical sciences; broader applicability is the
  authors' recommendation rather than a reported cross-field validation (“Road
  to consensus,” p. 211).
- It can be difficult to distinguish a predatory journal from a
  resource-constrained journal, and individual warning signs require cautious
  assessment (“Details matter” and “Criteria we left out,” p. 212).
- Intent to deceive, journal quality, and adequacy of peer review were excluded
  because the group considered them difficult or too subjective to assess with
  available information (“Criteria we left out,” p. 212).
- The group still planned to solicit input, make the definition usable, create
  an observatory, and develop and test an assessment tool, indicating that the
  published definition was a foundation for operational work rather than a
  completed classifier (“Next steps,” p. 212).

### Reviewer assessment

- Under the Academic Research Suite hierarchy, this *Nature* Comment is Level
  VII expert or committee evidence. It is overall Grade B for the bounded claim
  that a diverse 43-person group reached this wording through the reported
  modified Delphi process; it is not fit to support prevalence, journal-level
  classification, index-fairness, or visibility-effect claims.
- The Comment does not report how participants were selected, how countries or
  stakeholder groups were distributed, whether anyone withdrew, what consensus
  threshold applied, or how responses changed across rounds. The process is
  therefore not independently reproducible from the article.
- The definition's self-interest condition is not paired with an observable
  test, while the “and/or” practice categories do not state how much evidence is
  enough. Applying it as a selection boundary requires a later operational and
  validation study.
- The multi-stakeholder panel included publishing organizations, funders,
  institutions, and other interested parties. That breadth is relevant to
  consensus legitimacy but is not independent empirical validation; no
  competing-interest statement appears in the three-page PDF.
- The DOI, title, authorship, article type, date, volume, issue, and page range
  were verified against the publisher record and DOI metadata. Full text was
  read from a publicly accessible publisher PDF hosted by the University of
  Warwick.

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** A practice-based conceptual boundary that distinguishes
  misleading representation, publishing-integrity deficiencies, opacity, and
  solicitation conduct from OA, APCs, resource constraints, list status, and
  peer-review quality alone.
- **Project interpretation:** Use the definition as a bounded journal-selection
  rationale: evidence of specified practices can justify closer integrity
  scrutiny, but an OA model, index absence, newness, poor web design, or low
  resources cannot by themselves admit a journal to a “predatory” category.
  This boundary qualifies legitimacy-label mechanisms by requiring practice
  evidence before the label is treated as defensible.
- **Does not establish:** The prevalence of deceptive or deficient practices;
  whether any named journal satisfies the boundary; whether index admission or
  exclusion is fair; whether a predatory label is accurate in practice; whether
  labeling or journal conduct changes discoverability, readership, citations,
  submissions, evaluation, funding, policy use, or professional uptake; or
  whether OA, APC-funded, Global South, multilingual, community-owned, or OJS
  journals are disproportionately affected.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Journal-selection boundaries; deceptive representation;
  publishing-integrity practices; transparency; resource constraint; OA and APC
  separation; index and list disagreement; legitimacy labels; unmeasured
  visibility outcomes.
- **Agreements or tensions:** Krawczyk and Kulczycki (2021) show historically
  that scholarly discourse often joined OA and predation while still recording
  genuine integrity concerns. Grudniewicz et al. supply a later consensus
  boundary that does not use OA or APC status as a defining trait and therefore
  strengthens the case against that conflation. It does not refute Krawczyk and
  Kulczycki's discourse evidence, validate the accuracy of any label, or measure
  its visibility effects. Retaining the term “predatory” and placing the burden
  of proof on journals also leaves room for the legitimacy-label mechanism they
  identify when broad categories are applied without operational validation.

## Open questions

- Which operational indicators and thresholds can apply this consensus boundary
  reproducibly without converting resource constraint, OA, APCs, or index
  absence into proxies for misconduct?
- What journal-level sampling frame and denominator would estimate the
  prevalence of each deceptive or integrity-related practice under a validated
  operationalization?
- What evidence measures false inclusion and exclusion across indexes or lists,
  and whether errors vary by region, language, ownership, resources, or access
  model?
- Which designs directly test whether journal conduct or a “predatory” label
  changes index admission, discoverability, readership, citation, submission,
  evaluation, funding, policy use, or professional uptake?
