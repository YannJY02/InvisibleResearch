# Krawczyk and Kulczycki (2021) — Open access and predatory labeling

- **Citation:** Krawczyk, F., & Kulczycki, E. (2021). How is open access
  accused of being predatory? The impact of Beall's lists of predatory
  journals on academic publishing. *The Journal of Academic Librarianship,
  47*(2), 102271. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.acalib.2020.102271
- **Zotero:** [Open source item in Zotero](zotero://select/library/items/AIZDXRIM)
- **DOI or URL:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.acalib.2020.102271
- **Reviewed:** 2026-07-20
- **Reading basis:** Full text, PDF pp. 1–11; public appendix listing the 280
  analyzed publications

## Why this source matters

Krawczyk and Kulczycki examine how scholarly writing about predatory
publishing has joined, or kept separate, two different concerns: access to
research and validation of knowledge. Their coded review shows that Jeffrey
Beall's definitions and lists strongly shaped later descriptions of predatory
journals, including descriptions that make open access (OA), author charges,
and predation appear inseparable. For this project, the paper supplies evidence
for a legitimacy-label mechanism while also preserving genuine publishing-
integrity failures as a competing explanation.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Investigate how authors characterize predatory journals,
  with particular attention to where predatory publishing and OA are conflated
  or distinguished (Introduction, PDF pp. 1–2).
- **Conceptual framing:** The paper separates the OA problem of access to
  scientific knowledge from the knowledge-validation problem of identifying
  illegitimate methods, pseudoscience, deception, and deficient peer review.
  It treats “predatory publishing” as a discursive junction through which those
  problems became connected after Beall's interventions (Introduction and
  Discussion and conclusion, PDF pp. 1–2, 9).
- **Contribution claimed:** The authors present the first large-scale study to
  examine systematically how predatory publishing is defined in the scholarly
  literature and how Beall's approach influenced that discussion (Abstract;
  Introduction, PDF pp. 1–2).

## Methods

- **Design:** Systematic search and screening followed by qualitative content
  analysis and descriptive frequency analysis of coded publications (Materials
  and methods, PDF pp. 2–4).
- **Measures or classifications:** The authors classified included works as
  Beall, empirical, or theoretical publications. Nine reported codes cover
  whether predatory publishing was treated as new, deceptive, deficient in peer
  review, associated with author charges, combined with OA, or criticized for
  being combined with OA, plus publish-or-perish and attitudes toward Beall
  (Table 1, PDF p. 4).
- **Analysis:** The first author coded all 280 full texts in MaxQDA 12. Close
  reading identified themes and strategies; code frequencies were used to show
  their prevalence across publication categories. Non-Beall empirical works
  were also divided by whether Beall's lists were a main source, a supplementary
  source, or not used (Qualitative analysis; Tables 2, 4–6, PDF pp. 4–8).
- **Validation or robustness:** Both authors tested an initial 22-code scheme on
  several papers, discussed it, and revised difficult operationalizations before
  the first author applied the final scheme. The article provides its search
  terms, screening flow, nine reported code definitions, and a public appendix
  listing the final corpus; it does not report an intercoder-reliability statistic
  or publish the item-level coding matrix (Identification of papers; Qualitative
  analysis; Table 1, PDF pp. 3–4; Figshare appendix).

## Findings

### Beall's framing permeated later descriptions of predatory publishing

- **Source claim:** Beall's lists and characteristic themes had a profound
  influence on subsequent empirical and theoretical literature (Results;
  Discussion and conclusion, PDF pp. 4–9).
- **Evidence:** Beall was mentioned in 205 of 246 non-Beall publications (83%).
  Of 111 empirical studies, 48 were based on Beall's lists, 26 used them as a
  supplementary source, and 37 did not use them. Across empirical studies,
  64% combined predatory publishing with OA; the corresponding share among
  theoretical publications was 50% (Tables 2 and 4; “No better list is
  available so far,” PDF pp. 5–8).
- **Locator:** Tables 2 and 4; Strategies of combining predatory journals and
  open access publishing in non-Beall publications; “No better list is
  available so far,” PDF pp. 5–8.
- **Caveat:** Code presence shows how publications described the issue; it does
  not validate the accuracy of the descriptions or measure their effects on
  journals, authors, readers, citations, or indexes.

### The corpus contains systematic conflation as well as explicit separation

- **Source claim:** Many publications linked OA and predation through strategies
  similar to Beall's, but the literature was not uniform and included explicit
  critiques of that linkage (Strategies of combining predatory journals and
  open access publishing in non-Beall publications, PDF pp. 6–8).
- **Evidence:** Among 246 non-Beall publications, 92 combined predatory
  publishing with OA without a recorded critique and 30 combined them while
  partly criticizing the connection; these two categories produce the paper's
  reported total of 122. Fifty publications criticized combining predatory
  publishing with OA without themselves making that combination. The authors
  identify three modes of separation: extending scrutiny to unethical or
  profit-seeking subscription publishers, rejecting the generalization of some
  journals' failures to an entire access model, and criticizing Beall's negative
  bias toward OA (Table 5 and accompanying analysis, PDF pp. 7–8; Discussion
  and conclusion, PDF p. 9).
- **Locator:** Table 5 and accompanying text, PDF pp. 7–8; Discussion and
  conclusion, PDF p. 9.
- **Caveat:** The categories rest on the authors' qualitative judgments, and the
  published appendix lists sources but not their item-level category or code
  assignments.

### Conflation operated through identifiable rhetorical and classificatory strategies

- **Source claim:** Beall joined predation to OA through five recurrent
  strategies that, except for his most radical attacks on OA advocates, also
  appeared widely in non-Beall publications (Jeffrey Beall on predatory open
  access publishing; Discussion and conclusion, PDF pp. 5–9).
- **Evidence:** The strategies were defining predatory publishing as OA;
  praising traditional publishers while treating their failures as exceptions;
  blaming OA advocates; presenting internal tensions between OA's benefits and
  risks; and generalizing features of some fee-charging OA journals to OA as a
  whole. One key definitional move equated gold OA with author charges, omitting
  non-APC gold OA and making the author-pays incentive appear intrinsic to the
  whole model (Strategies 1–5, PDF pp. 5–7).
- **Locator:** Strategies 1–5, PDF pp. 5–7; Table 3, PDF p. 5.
- **Caveat:** The authors say not every connection is invalid. In particular,
  debate about APC incentives and unequal ability to pay remains legitimate;
  their objection is to treating those concerns as properties of all OA
  publishing (Discussion and conclusion, PDF p. 9).

### Predatory labels may shape recognition, but this study does not measure visibility effects

- **Source claim:** Overgeneralizing failures from some OA journals creates
  unjustified prejudice toward OA, while positive portrayals of Beall and the
  routine use of his lists make his assumptions appear objective and less
  visible as contestable judgments (Abstract; “No better list is available so
  far”; Discussion and conclusion, PDF pp. 8–9).
- **Evidence:** A positive or mixed positive-and-negative attitude toward Beall
  appeared in 127 of 246 non-Beall publications. The authors describe a
  positive feedback loop in which praise for Beall strengthens the authority of
  the OA–predation connection, and the established connection then supports
  continued reliance on his lists (Table 6 and accompanying text, PDF pp. 8–9).
- **Locator:** Table 6 and accompanying text, PDF pp. 8–9; Discussion and
  conclusion, PDF p. 9.
- **Caveat:** The paper analyzes discourse. It does not test whether a label
  changes index inclusion, discoverability, citation rates, submission choices,
  hiring or funding evaluation, policy use, or professional uptake. Any pathway
  from stigma or blacklist inclusion to scholarly invisibility is therefore a
  project inference requiring outcome evidence.

### Real integrity failures remain a competing explanation, not a dismissed concern

- **Source claim:** Poor or absent peer review, deceptive intent, fake editorial
  boards, misleading metrics, spam, pseudoscientific content, and exploitation
  of author charges are genuine knowledge-validation problems; the error is to
  define them as inherent to OA or to assume a blacklist settles their presence
  (Introduction; Discussion and conclusion, PDF pp. 1–2, 9–10).
- **Evidence:** Poor peer review was coded in 77% of empirical and 60% of
  theoretical publications; intent to deceive in 51% and 36%, respectively;
  and author charges in 68% and 64%. The authors support more transparent,
  rigorous tools for investigating journals with poor or absent publishing
  standards rather than abandoning integrity screening (Tables 2 and 4;
  Discussion and conclusion, PDF pp. 5, 9–10).
- **Locator:** Introduction, PDF pp. 1–2; Tables 2 and 4, PDF pp. 5, 7;
  Discussion and conclusion, PDF pp. 9–10.
- **Caveat:** These frequencies record claims in the reviewed literature, not
  verified prevalence estimates of misconduct. This paper does not audit a
  sample of journals or compare the integrity of OA and subscription venues.

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- The final corpus excludes non-English publications, popular and magazine
  articles, blogs, and purely informational scholarly publications. The authors
  argue that contributory scholarly publications should be more reflexive, but
  acknowledge that their inference to excluded texts is an assumption
  (Discussion and conclusion, PDF p. 10).
- The analyzed literature stops before December 2018. The authors note that the
  later consensus definition by Grudniewicz et al. (2019) does not mention OA,
  while also citing a 2020 identification study that still defines predatory
  journals through exploitation of gold OA (Discussion and conclusion, PDF p.
  10).

### Reviewer assessment

- Under the Academic Research Suite hierarchy, this is Level V evidence: a
  systematic qualitative review of descriptive and conceptual publications.
  It is overall Grade B for claims about pre-December-2018 English-language
  discourse and should be used only as conceptual, not outcome or causal,
  evidence for scholarly visibility.
- A single researcher coded the full corpus. Joint code development and pilot
  discussion improve interpretive consistency, but the absence of double coding
  and intercoder reliability leaves category boundaries dependent on one
  coder's judgments (Qualitative analysis, PDF p. 4).
- The expert-list seed and automatic inclusion of Beall's 34 publications
  ensured that Beall was prominent in the corpus. The more probative influence
  findings concern the 246 non-Beall publications, especially list use and code
  frequencies reported separately from Beall's work (Identification of papers;
  Tables 2, 4–6, PDF pp. 3, 5–8).
- The public appendix verifies the identities and three broad classes of the 280
  sources but does not expose the item-level nine-code matrix, coded excerpts,
  or database-specific adjusted search strings. Independent reproduction of the
  reported frequencies is therefore not possible from the shared materials
  alone.
- The DOI, title, authors, 2021 issue metadata, CC BY Version of Record, and
  public appendix were verified during this review. The source reports National
  Science Centre funding and declares no competing interest (Funding and
  Declaration of competing interest, PDF p. 10).

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** Treating “predatory” as a potentially consequential
  legitimacy label rather than a neutral journal property; separating OA model,
  APC model, peer-review integrity, deceptive conduct, and blacklist status;
  and retaining journal-integrity failures as a competing explanation that
  requires transparent evidence.
- **Project interpretation:** A plausible visibility pathway is that an
  authoritative blacklist or repeated OA–predation association lowers perceived
  legitimacy, which can discourage reading, citation, submission, evaluation,
  or index consideration. The paper establishes the upstream discourse and
  classification mechanism, not those downstream outcomes. The project should
  therefore code this as conceptual evidence about recognition and labeling,
  paired with later empirical work on actual visibility effects and false
  positives across regions and languages.
- **Does not establish:** That OA journals are generally high or low quality;
  that Beall-listed journals are all legitimate or illegitimate; the prevalence
  of integrity failures; a causal effect of labels on citation or index
  inclusion; disproportionate labeling of Global South, multilingual, or OJS
  journals; or that every integrity-screening system reproduces exclusion.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Legitimacy and stigma; category conflation; blacklist authority;
  OA and APC distinctions; peer-review integrity; pseudoscience and knowledge
  validation; recognition as a possible precursor to scholarly visibility.
- **Agreements or tensions:** The discourse mechanism sharpens Khanna et al.'s
  warning that broad predatory framing can peripheralize a diverse OA publishing
  stratum, but it also limits that warning: Krawczyk and Kulczycki affirm real
  integrity threats and do not show that any specific OJS journal was falsely
  labeled. Their results therefore challenge both wholesale suspicion of OA and
  wholesale dismissal of predatory-publishing concerns.

## Open questions

- Do post-2018 definitions and screening tools separate deceptive conduct from
  OA and APC status more consistently?
- Which studies directly measure whether blacklist inclusion or a predatory
  label changes submissions, readership, citations, index decisions,
  institutional evaluation, or policy and professional use?
- How often do blacklist false positives or ambiguous classifications affect
  Global South, multilingual, community-owned, or resource-constrained journals
  compared with otherwise similar journals?
- Can the authors' item-level coding matrix or coded excerpts be obtained to
  reproduce the 92/30/50 categorization and test borderline cases?
