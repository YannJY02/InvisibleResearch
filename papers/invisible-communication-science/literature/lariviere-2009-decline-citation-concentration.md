# Larivière et al. (2009) — Decline in citation concentration

- **Citation:** Larivière, V., Gingras, Y., & Archambault, É. (2009). The decline in the concentration of citations, 1900–2007. *Journal of the American Society for Information Science and Technology, 60*(4), 858–862. https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.21011
- **Zotero:** [Open source item in Zotero](zotero://select/library/items/9WKD3ILF)
- **DOI or URL:** https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.21011
- **Reviewed:** 2026-07-20
- **Reading basis:** Full text, pp. 858–862

## Why this source matters

Larivière et al. provide a direct empirical counterweight to claims that
scholarly attention is becoming steadily more concentrated. Across three broad
Web of Science fields, more papers received citations and a larger share of
papers accounted for fixed shares of citations over time. The result belongs in
this corpus because it rules out a simple system-wide concentration story while
also showing why aggregate citation dispersion cannot establish equal
visibility across journals, languages, countries, institutions, authors, or
scholarly communities.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Test whether the concentration of citations received by
  papers increased as scholarly literature moved online, as Evans (2008)
  reported, or instead declined over the long run (pp. 858–859).
- **Conceptual framing:** Citation concentration is treated as a distributional
  property of papers published in the same year. Greater citedness, a larger
  share of papers needed to accumulate fixed citation shares, and a lower
  Herfindahl–Hirschman index (HHI) each indicate greater dispersion (p. 859).
- **Contribution claimed:** Three measures converge at the macro level: citation
  concentration declined in natural sciences and engineering, medical fields,
  and social sciences, contradicting a general narrowing account and suggesting
  a more broadly used stock of published knowledge (pp. 861–862).

## Data and scope

| Element | Source-reported detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Papers published in a given year and the citations they received; the principal analysis is aggregated into four broad fields (pp. 859–861). |
| Data source | Thomson Scientific Web of Science: Science Citation Index Expanded, Social Sciences Citation Index, and Arts and Humanities Citation Index (p. 859). |
| Sample and coverage | More than 27 million articles, notes, and reviews—11 million in natural sciences and engineering, 12.7 million in medical fields, 2.5 million in social sciences, and 0.9 million in humanities—matched to more than 615 million references; first-author self-citations were excluded (p. 859). |
| Time period | Source data cover 1900–2007. Complete publication cohorts end in 2005 for the 2-year citation window and 2002 for the 5-year window; natural sciences and engineering and medical fields begin in 1900, social sciences in 1956, and humanities in 1975 (p. 859). |
| Geographic or linguistic scope | Not reported. The database is international, but results are not disaggregated by country, region, language, institution, author, journal, or scholarly community. |

## Methods

- **Design:** Longitudinal descriptive bibliometric analysis of citation
  distributions by publication year and broad field (pp. 859–861).
- **Measures or classifications:** The study uses (1) the percentage of papers
  cited within 2 and 5 years; (2) the percentage of papers needed to account for
  20%, 50%, and 80% of citations received by a publication-year cohort; and (3)
  the HHI calculated from each paper's share of citations within its cohort.
  Journals were grouped through a U.S. National Science Foundation taxonomy,
  supplemented by an in-house humanities classification (p. 859).
- **Analysis:** Trends are plotted separately for natural sciences and
  engineering, medical fields, social sciences, and humanities. The authors
  compare field trajectories, wartime and post-1990 changes, and 2- versus
  5-year citation windows (pp. 860–861).
- **Validation or robustness:** The three concentration measures converge, and
  the authors report that an unshown 10-year citedness window, reference-made
  analysis, and journal-level HHI analysis follow the same trend (pp. 860–861).
  The article does not report uncertainty intervals, model-based robustness
  tests, or the underlying annual values.

## Findings

### A growing share of indexed papers received at least one citation

- **Source claim:** Outside the humanities, fewer published papers went
  unnoticed within fixed citation windows over the long run (p. 860).
- **Evidence:** For papers published in 2005, the 2-year cited shares reached
  about 80% in medical fields, 60% in natural sciences and engineering, and 55%
  in social sciences. With a 5-year window for 2002 cohorts, the uncited shares
  were 12%, 27%, and 32%, respectively (Figure 1, pp. 859–860).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 1, pp. 859–860.
- **Caveat:** Receiving one citation is a low threshold and does not measure the
  amount, source, prestige, or practical use of attention. The humanities do
  not follow the pattern, partly because journal articles omit much book-based
  citation activity (p. 860).

### The post-1990 distribution broadened in three broad fields

- **Source claim:** A larger percentage of papers came to account for fixed
  shares of citations, indicating declining concentration in natural sciences
  and engineering, medical fields, and social sciences (pp. 860–861).
- **Evidence:** In 2005, 33% of medical papers and 28% of natural sciences and
  engineering papers accounted for 80% of citations, compared with 24% and 23%
  in 1990. In social sciences, the equivalent share rose from 14% in 1956 and
  19% in 1990 to 28% in 2005. Natural sciences and engineering and medical
  fields first became more concentrated from roughly the 1960s to 1990 before
  dispersion resumed; social-science dispersion increased continuously from
  1956 and faster after 1990 (Figure 2, p. 860).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 2, p. 860.
- **Caveat:** The trends remain aggregate field averages. They do not show
  whether citation shares became more equal between regions, languages,
  institutions, journals, authors, or subfields.

### Humanities remain an explicit exception

- **Source claim:** Journal-based citation data show no comparable dispersion
  trend in the humanities and require extreme caution for research evaluation
  (p. 860).
- **Evidence:** In 2005, only 0.5% of humanities papers accounted for 20% of
  citations, 2.6% accounted for 50%, and 7.2% accounted for 80%; apart from an
  early-1980s bump attributed to poor data, no trend was discernible (p. 860).
- **Locator:** Results, p. 860.
- **Caveat:** The humanities series begins only in 1975, covers journal-indexed
  papers, and omits the books that the authors identify as central to humanities
  referencing (pp. 859–860).

### HHI confirms lower macro-level concentration but is sensitive to system growth

- **Source claim:** The HHI of citations received declined substantially over
  time in all fields except humanities, with temporary increases during the two
  World Wars and around the end of the 1980s (p. 861).
- **Evidence:** Both 2- and 5-year HHI series trend downward; the authors report
  that 2005 papers had the lowest concentration in the history of natural
  sciences and engineering, medical fields, and social sciences. They also say
  that unshown reference-made and journal-level HHI calculations produce the
  same direction (Figure 3, p. 861).
- **Locator:** Results, Figure 3, p. 861.
- **Caveat:** The authors state that the HHI decline “simply reflects” the
  exponential increase in papers published and cited and note its sensitivity
  to the number of competing units (p. 861). Dispersion therefore need not imply
  the disappearance of cumulative advantage or subgroup inequality.

### Dispersion is a genuine but bounded counter-explanation

- **Source claim:** The observed aggregate trend is inconsistent with an
  increasingly exclusive and elitist scientific system and can be interpreted
  as increasing efficiency and democratization in the use of published
  knowledge (p. 861).
- **Evidence:** Citedness, fixed-share concentration, and HHI all move toward
  broader article-level citation distributions in the three non-humanities
  fields, despite different measures and citation windows (pp. 859–861).
- **Locator:** Discussion and Conclusion, pp. 861–862.
- **Caveat:** “Democratic” is an interpretation of within-database aggregate
  dispersion, not a direct measure of equal recognition. The study cannot show
  whether groups absent from Web of Science gained visibility or whether
  between-group disparities narrowed while within-group citation spread grew.

### The study does not identify online access as the cause

- **Source claim:** Evans's reported concentration effect is either marginal or
  an artefact at the macro level, but this study cannot test whether online
  availability caused either concentration or dispersion (p. 861).
- **Evidence:** The analysis contains no online-availability variable. The
  authors instead compare the direction of their macro trends with Evans's
  finding and propose a possible timing problem in his measurement (pp.
  861–862).
- **Locator:** Discussion and Conclusion, pp. 861–862.
- **Caveat:** The design establishes a descriptive temporal trend, not a causal
  effect of electronic publishing, search, database coverage, or author
  behaviour.

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- The online-availability variable is absent, so the analysis cannot prove that
  electronic publishing or access drives the observed trend (p. 861).
- HHI is highly sensitive to the number of competing papers, and its long-run
  decline reflects growth in the number of papers published and cited (p. 861).
- Journal-based data poorly represent humanities communication because books
  receive a substantial share of humanities references (p. 860).
- Social-science and humanities coverage begins much later than coverage of
  natural sciences and engineering and medical fields (p. 859).

### Reviewer assessment

- Web of Science defines the observable system. A broader citation distribution
  among indexed papers can coexist with persistent exclusion of nonindexed,
  regional, multilingual, or community-based publishing; the study therefore
  cannot adjudicate Khanna et al.'s index-coverage claim.
- Four broad fields can conceal subfield and subgroup inequality. Inferring
  equal author-, institution-, country-, language-, or journal-level visibility
  from paper-level aggregate trends would be an ecological fallacy; aggregation
  could also hide opposing subgroup trends.
- Fixed 2- and 5-year windows favor fields with faster journal citation cycles.
  The first-author self-citation exclusion does not remove all self-citations,
  and the article reports no sensitivity check for that choice (p. 859).
- Figure 3 and its surrounding prose say wartime concentration increased, but a
  following sentence says the smaller paper pool “diminish[ed] the HHI”; because
  higher HHI denotes greater concentration, that sentence appears internally
  inconsistent and should not be reused as a directional explanation without
  checking the underlying data (p. 861).
- The study measures scholarly citation only. It does not observe discovery,
  prestige, evaluation rewards, policy influence, teaching, professional use,
  or within-community circulation.

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** A real long-run countertrend to the claim that citation
  attention is inevitably concentrating on fewer papers; the importance of
  distinguishing paper-level citation dispersion from other kinds and units of
  scholarly visibility; and the need to preserve field and period boundaries.
- **Project interpretation:** The initial corpus should treat system-level
  dispersion as a competing explanation, not a dismissal of systemic
  invisibility. A credible later synthesis can ask whether broader citation
  participation within dominant indexes coexists with unequal admission to
  those indexes and unequal recognition between regions, languages,
  institutions, journals, authors, or communities.
- **Does not establish:** Equal scholarly visibility; the decline of
  center–periphery, linguistic, infrastructural, or prestige inequalities; a
  causal effect of online access; current citation patterns after 2007; or
  dispersion outside Web of Science. It does not grant this note or the corpus
  Paper Analysis authority.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Citation concentration and dispersion; uncitedness; cumulative
  advantage; online access and search; field differences; unit-of-analysis and
  aggregation boundaries; competing explanations for unequal visibility.
- **Agreements or tensions:** Larivière et al. directly challenge a general
  narrowing narrative and require any systemic-invisibility account to allow
  aggregate democratizing trends. They do not conflict directly with Khanna et
  al. (2022): Larivière et al. study citation distributions inside Web of
  Science, whereas Khanna et al. show that much OJS publishing is absent from
  selective indexes. The two findings can coexist, and their tension becomes an
  empirical question about within-index dispersion versus between-group and
  admission inequalities.

## Open questions

- Do later studies replicate declining article-level concentration after 2007
  with open, cross-index data and subgroup decompositions?
- Does citation dispersion increase within regions, languages, institutions,
  journals, and author groups, or does macro dispersion coexist with stable or
  widening between-group inequality?
- How much of the HHI trend remains after separating the mechanical effect of
  publication growth from changes in citing behaviour?
- Do within-community citation, policy use, teaching use, or professional uptake
  show the same dispersion pattern as Web of Science citations?
