# Ondari-Okemwa (2007) — Scholarly publishing in sub-Saharan Africa

- **Citation:** Ondari-Okemwa, E. (2007). Scholarly publishing in sub-Saharan Africa in the twenty-first century: Challenges and opportunities. *First Monday, 12*(10). https://doi.org/10.5210/fm.v12i10.1966
- **Zotero:** [Open source item in Zotero](zotero://select/library/items/6QRQ7UK8)
- **DOI or URL:** https://doi.org/10.5210/fm.v12i10.1966
- **Reviewed:** 2026-07-20
- **Reading basis:** Full-text HTML, all named sections and Tables 2–3

## Why this source matters

Ondari-Okemwa provides a historical account of how limited research resources,
communication infrastructure, institutional freedom, language, and scholarly
attention may constrain publishing and visibility in sub-Saharan Africa. Its
publication counts offer direct evidence about records present in three Thomson
Scientific indexes in 1997–May 2007. The proposed mechanisms are useful
literature-review leads, but most are argued from prior literature and examples
rather than tested against those publication records.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Examine publications produced by scholars based in
  sub-Saharan Africa from 1997 to 2007; identify the roles, challenges, and
  causes associated with scholarly publishing; and propose ways to improve
  knowledge production and dissemination (section “Purpose of this study”).
- **Conceptual framing:** Scholarly publishing is treated as both an indicator
  of knowledge production and a means of knowledge diffusion, professional
  evaluation, collaboration, and participation in a digitizing global economy
  (sections “Introduction” and “Scholarly publishing”). Visibility moves among
  indexed output, journal prestige, citation attention, and access to
  electronic communication; the paper does not operationally separate them.
- **Contribution claimed:** The author combines a descriptive count of
  Thomson-indexed publications with an account of economic, technological,
  socio-political, linguistic, and institutional constraints, then identifies
  electronic publishing and collaboration as possible opportunities (Abstract;
  sections “Results and discussion,” “Challenges of scholarly publishing in
  sub-Saharan Africa,” and “Conclusion”).

## Data and scope

| Element | Source-reported detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Publication records attributed to countries through author institutional address or country fields in Thomson Scientific indexes (section “Methodology”). |
| Data source | Science Citation Index, Social Sciences Citation Index, and Arts and Humanities Citation Index; records downloaded as text and analyzed by country (section “Methodology”). |
| Sample and coverage | Twenty-eight sub-Saharan African countries with at least 235 indexed records in 1997–2007; a separate 2006 comparison used selected countries outside the region (section “Methodology”; Tables 2–3). |
| Time period | 1997 through May 2007 for the regional table; 2006 for the cross-region comparison (section “Methodology”; notes to Tables 2–3). |
| Geographic or linguistic scope | Authors affiliated with institutions in sub-Saharan Africa; citizens of the region residing elsewhere were excluded. The challenge discussion generalizes across the region and also addresses English, French, Portuguese, Kiswahili, and Afrikaans (sections “Methodology” and “Language challenges”). |

## Methods

- **Design:** Descriptive bibliometric count followed by a narrative discussion
  of challenges and opportunities.
- **Measures or classifications:** Country output was retrieved with
  institutional-address (`AD`) or country (`CU`) searches. Only countries with
  at least 235 records were retained for the regional analysis. The paper does
  not define a measure for technological, economic, political, environmental,
  linguistic, or citation-attention constraints (section “Methodology”).
- **Analysis:** Records were downloaded and counted by country. The regional
  totals were compared descriptively with 2006 totals for selected countries
  elsewhere; the challenge taxonomy was developed narratively from cited work
  and illustrative cases (sections “Results and discussion” and “Challenges of
  scholarly publishing in sub-Saharan Africa”).
- **Validation or robustness:** Not reported. The author states that the counts
  are not absolute because some regional publications appear in journals not
  indexed by Thomson Scientific (section “Results and discussion”).

## Findings

### Indexed output was low relative to selected comparators and highly uneven within the region

- **Source claim:** The region's low indexed publication count indicates a
  problem in scholarly publishing and knowledge production, while South Africa
  accounts for a large share of the recorded output.
- **Evidence:** Table 2 reports 51,738 records for South Africa, 9,540 for
  Nigeria, 6,661 for Kenya, and 235 for the Democratic Republic of Congo across
  1997–May 2007. Table 3 reports more than 100,000 U.S. records and 97,904
  English records for 2006 alone.
- **Locator:** Section “Results and discussion”; Tables 2–3.
- **Caveat:** The data measure presence in three selective indexes, not all
  scholarship produced. The comparison is not normalized for population,
  institutions, fields, or research spending, and the 2007 count is partial.

### Resource scarcity constrains both research production and routes to publication

- **Source claim:** Underfunded universities, libraries, laboratories, and
  scholars reduce the research and communication capacity from which
  publications emerge.
- **Evidence:** The discussion describes library budget cuts and journal
  cancellations, outdated collections, poorly equipped laboratories, costly
  or unreliable Internet and electricity, weak publication incentives,
  unaffordable conference participation, and brain drain associated with poor
  pay and working conditions.
- **Locator:** Subsections “Economic-related challenges,” “Lack of incentives,”
  “Non-participation in scholarly conferences,” and “Brain drain.”
- **Caveat:** These mechanisms are not measured in the bibliometric analysis.
  They are a narrative synthesis of cited sources and examples, with no
  cross-country test linking resource differences to publication or citation
  outcomes.

### Digital publishing is both an opportunity and an exclusion mechanism

- **Source claim:** Electronic journals can expand access, submission, peer
  review, and collaboration, but unequal access to computers, e-mail,
  telecommunications, electricity, and the Internet can reproduce or widen the
  knowledge divide.
- **Evidence:** The paper describes universities unable to maintain continuous
  connectivity and scholars unable to participate in electronic submission,
  reading, or reviewing; it contrasts those conditions with the potential for
  online collaboration and rapid exchange.
- **Locator:** Subsections “Technological challenges” and “Electronic journals”;
  section “Conclusion.”
- **Caveat:** No infrastructure indicators or user-level access data are
  analyzed. The claims describe the early-2000s context and cannot be treated as
  a current regional estimate.

### The paper's “environmental” constraint is primarily political and institutional

- **Source claim:** Restricted expression, censorship, politically controlled
  universities, and career or legal retaliation discourage scholars from
  publishing critical research and shape what knowledge is produced.
- **Evidence:** The paper discusses government appointment of university
  leaders, self-censorship among humanities and social-science scholars, risks
  to promotion and employment, and a Kenyan case in which politically favored
  research received support while sensitive topics were discouraged.
- **Locator:** Subsection “Environmental challenges.”
- **Caveat:** “Environmental” does not refer to ecological or climate
  conditions. The evidence is illustrative rather than a systematic comparison
  of censorship, university autonomy, or publication behavior across the
  region.

### Language can affect admission to journals and participation in scholarly communication

- **Source claim:** Requiring scholars to publish in English, French, or
  Portuguese can exclude participation and lead editors to reject substantively
  useful manuscripts for language reasons; regional languages could support
  additional scholarly circuits.
- **Evidence:** The discussion notes that many scholars use the dominant
  publication languages as second languages, cites grammar-related manuscript
  rejection, and contrasts this with the limited scholarly use of Kiswahili and
  Afrikaans.
- **Locator:** Subsection “Language challenges.”
- **Caveat:** The study does not analyze manuscript decisions, language-specific
  publication rates, translation, or readership. The proposed mechanism remains
  a source lead rather than a measured effect.

### Visibility is also framed as an attention and citation-network problem

- **Source claim:** Work by scholars based in sub-Saharan Africa can remain
  little noticed or cited even when it appears in prestigious Northern
  journals, partly because scholars elsewhere may not study the region.
- **Evidence:** The paper distinguishes the visibility of articles and journals
  from their mere publication and argues that outside researchers who work on
  sub-Saharan Africa are more likely to cite regional scholarship.
- **Locator:** Subsection “Invisibility of scholarly publications emanating
  from research conducted in sub-Saharan Africa.”
- **Caveat:** No citation data, audience study, or comparison of article quality
  is presented. Journal selection, field composition, topic relevance, language
  networks, and locally oriented use remain alternatives.

### Collaboration and shared acquisition are proposed responses, not evaluated interventions

- **Source claim:** Cross-regional and within-region co-publishing, electronic
  peer review, library purchasing consortia, and inter-library loans could
  enlarge access and participation.
- **Evidence:** The conclusion connects knowledge production to collaboration
  and proposes shared library acquisition and lending to reduce costs.
- **Locator:** Sections “Technological challenges” and “Conclusion.”
- **Caveat:** The paper does not evaluate whether these interventions increase
  publication, discoverability, citation, or professional use.

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- The indexed records “may not be absolute” because some publications from
  sub-Saharan Africa appear in journals outside the Thomson Scientific indexes
  (section “Results and discussion”).
- Authors from the region who resided elsewhere were excluded from the count
  (section “Methodology”).
- The 2007 values are based on records available in May rather than a complete
  publication year (note to Table 2).

### Reviewer assessment

- The publication-count analysis does not test the paper's economic,
  technological, political, environmental, linguistic, or attention-based
  mechanisms. For those claims, this is Level VI contextual evidence and is
  best used at Grade C with explicit caveats; the historical indexed counts are
  more directly supported.
- Index absence is partly built into the outcome: Thomson-based counts can
  reflect database coverage as well as underlying research production. This
  makes the paper evidence of both low indexed output and a measurement
  boundary, not a clean estimate of all regional scholarship.
- The 235-record threshold excludes the lowest-count countries, the comparison
  countries are described as random without a reproducible selection procedure,
  and output is not normalized for country size or research capacity.
- The methods name some comparison countries that do not appear in Table 3 and
  do not explain the discrepancy. The challenge discussion also cites Teferra
  (2004), but that item is absent from the reference list.
- Country-level heterogeneity is substantial, yet many narrative claims apply
  one regional description. Some background language is sweeping and dated and
  should not be reused as a characterization of present-day sub-Saharan Africa.
- The DOI, title, author, date, journal, and publisher were independently
  matched in Crossref and Semantic Scholar. OpenAlex identified the published
  article as open access and not retracted on the review date. First Monday
  describes its Articles section as peer reviewed; no author conflict of
  interest or funding disclosure was reported in the article.

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** A historically grounded mechanism inventory linking
  scholarly visibility to upstream research resources, access to literature,
  digital participation, institutional freedom, publication language, and
  citation attention. It also directly supports the warning that selective
  index counts omit some regional publishing.
- **Project interpretation:** The Khanna-derived infrastructure path should be
  wider than metadata interoperability alone: infrastructure can affect whether
  research is produced, submitted, reviewed, accessed, and cited. At the same
  time, Ondari-Okemwa's own index caveat anticipates Khanna et al.'s later
  argument that low database counts can conceal existing publishing rather than
  simply measure low production.
- **Does not establish:** Current infrastructure conditions, a causal effect of
  any proposed constraint, the total output of sub-Saharan Africa, the quality
  of included or excluded work, or equivalence among index inclusion, citation,
  prestige, and policy or professional use.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Research capacity; digital and library infrastructure;
  institutional freedom and censorship; language hierarchy; brain drain;
  selective-index measurement; citation attention; collaborative remedies.
- **Agreements or tensions:** Ondari-Okemwa and Khanna et al. agree that
  selective indexes do not exhaust regional scholarly publishing. They differ
  in emphasis: Ondari-Okemwa reads low Thomson counts partly as low production
  capacity, whereas Khanna et al. demonstrate a large publishing stratum hidden
  by selective indexes. These explanations can coexist, but neither paper
  identifies their relative contribution.

## Open questions

- Which of the paper's proposed mechanisms remain empirically supported after
  2007, and in which countries, disciplines, and institution types?
- How much of the observed output gap survives when coverage expands beyond
  Thomson indexes and output is normalized by population, researchers,
  institutions, field, and research spending?
- Can later work distinguish constraints on research production from
  constraints on submission, acceptance, discoverability, citation, prestige,
  and non-academic use?
- Do diaspora collaboration and local or regional circulation compensate for
  the affiliation and Northern-attention mechanisms proposed here?
- Which challenge claims trace to direct empirical evidence in the cited
  sources, and which are repeated expert judgments?
