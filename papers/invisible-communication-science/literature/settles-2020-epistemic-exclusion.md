# Settles et al. (2020) — Epistemic exclusion and intersectionality

- **Citation:** Settles, I. H., Warner, L. R., Buchanan, N. T., & Jones, M. K. (2020). Understanding psychology's resistance to intersectionality theory using a framework of epistemic exclusion and invisibility. *Journal of Social Issues, 76*(4), 796–813. https://doi.org/10.1111/josi.12403
- **Zotero:** [Open source item in Zotero](zotero://select/library/items/ZZNMH8ER)
- **DOI or URL:** https://doi.org/10.1111/josi.12403
- **Reviewed:** 2026-07-20
- **Reading basis:** Full published text, pp. 796–813, from the University of Michigan Deep Blue repository

## Why this source matters

Settles and colleagues offer a direct explanation for the devaluation of a
body of scholarship inside a discipline. Their framework joins disciplinary
bias about legitimate topics, methods, and epistemologies with identity bias
about who counts as a credible knower, then uses visibility theory to describe
how gatekeepers can render intersectionality absent, distorted, or superficially
prominent.

The paper is especially useful because it contains a source-specific descriptive
check of where intersectionality articles appeared in psychology journals. It
also requires careful qualification: the article is primarily conceptual, most
of its empirical support comes from cited studies, and its own publication
search does not observe editorial decisions, rejection rates, evaluator
reasoning, or the identities of article authors.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Explain the devaluation and marginalization of
  intersectionality theory in dominant psychology through epistemic exclusion
  and visibility theories; examine implications for social-issues scholarship;
  and recommend ways to increase epistemic inclusion (pp. 796–798).
- **Conceptual framing:** Epistemic exclusion is the devaluation of scholarship
  outside a disciplinary center and of marginalized scholars as credible
  contributors to knowledge. The authors propose two interacting sources:
  disciplinary bias about legitimate scholarship and identity-related bias
  about credible scholars (pp. 797–800). Visibility theory distinguishes
  accurate recognition from invisibility and hypervisibility and emphasizes
  power between perceivers and those being perceived (pp. 804–805).
- **Contribution claimed:** The paper applies both theories to psychology's
  resistance to intersectionality, distinguishes formal from informal
  exclusion, identifies three visibility processes through which dominant
  psychologists may preserve disciplinary boundaries, and proposes structural
  changes to journals, training, and disciplinary values (pp. 800–808).

## Data and scope

| Element | Source-reported detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Primarily intersectionality theory and scholarship within psychology. The embedded PsycINFO search analyzes journal articles; cited evidence also concerns faculty and NIH grant applicants (pp. 799–805). |
| Data source | Conceptual and empirical literature plus an author-conducted PsycINFO search for peer-reviewed journal articles using `intersectionality` as a search term (pp. 798–808). |
| Sample and coverage | The PsycINFO search found 1,642 peer-reviewed articles from 2004 through the first three quarters of 2019 and compared their placement in the 10, 20, and 30 psychology journals with the highest impact factors. It also classified 441 articles as qualitative (pp. 802–803). |
| Time period | The focal search covers 2004 through the first three quarters of 2019. Other evidence is drawn from prior studies cited through 2020 (pp. 802–803). |
| Geographic or linguistic scope | Not reported for the PsycINFO search. Funding examples concern the U.S. National Institutes of Health; the article discusses dominant psychology without defining a complete national or linguistic boundary (pp. 802–803). |

## Methods

- **Design:** Conceptual framework article with an embedded descriptive
  bibliographic search.
- **Measures or classifications:** Presence of the search term
  `intersectionality`; publication in the top 10, 20, or 30 psychology journals
  by impact factor; and classification as qualitative research. Formal and
  informal epistemic exclusion are otherwise developed through theory and
  examples from cited literature (pp. 799–805).
- **Analysis:** Descriptive counts and percentages from PsycINFO are interpreted
  alongside prior work on faculty evaluation, journal content, NIH grant
  funding, and applications of intersectionality. Visibility theory is then
  used to organize three proposed power processes (pp. 799–805).
- **Validation or robustness:** Not reported. The paper does not give the full
  PsycINFO query, search fields, ranking source and year, screening or coding
  procedure, duplicate handling, denominator comparisons, or sensitivity
  checks. It does not test whether search results change with related terms or
  with alternative definitions of mainstream journals (pp. 802–803).

## Findings

### Epistemic exclusion combines disciplinary and identity-related bias

- **Source claim:** Intersectionality is vulnerable to epistemic exclusion
  because it challenges psychology's dominant norms of objectivity,
  generalizability, researcher neutrality, quantitative measurement,
  parsimony, individual-level explanation, and value-free science, while many
  scholars who use it belong to marginalized groups (pp. 799–802).
- **Evidence:** The article connects prior conceptual and empirical research on
  marginalized topics, non-dominant methods, faculty evaluation, social-group
  stereotypes, and the underuse of innovations by marginalized scholars. It
  then shows how intersectionality differs from dominant psychological
  epistemologies and common factorial designs (pp. 799–802).
- **Locator:** “Epistemic exclusion as resulting from two types of bias” and
  “How intersectionality challenges psychology's disciplinary norms,”
  pp. 799–802.
- **Caveat:** The two-bias interaction is the paper's theoretical warrant. The
  focal article does not directly compare evaluations of otherwise identical
  intersectionality scholarship by topic, method, or author identity, and it
  does not estimate the contribution of each proposed source of bias.

### Intersectionality articles were concentrated outside the highest-impact psychology journals

- **Source claim:** Growth in intersectionality publications did not translate
  into proportional placement in the disciplinary outlets the authors treat as
  the mainstream center (p. 802).
- **Evidence:** The authors' PsycINFO search found 1,642 peer-reviewed articles
  from 2004 through the first three quarters of 2019. Of these, 0.4% appeared
  in the 10 psychology journals with the highest impact factors, 1.1% in the
  top 20, and 2.9% in the top 30. Within the top 30, *Journal of Counseling
  Psychology* contained 20 intersectionality articles and *American
  Psychologist* contained 10; the authors report that most such scholarship
  appeared in specialty, interdisciplinary, or non-psychology journals
  (p. 802).
- **Locator:** “Evidence for the formal epistemic exclusion of Intersectionality
  theory,” p. 802.
- **Caveat:** These are publication shares among retrieved intersectionality
  articles, not submission, rejection, acceptance, readership, or citation
  rates. The paper supplies no baseline share for all psychology articles or
  for other theories, so the observed concentration does not by itself identify
  editorial gatekeeping, author self-selection, journal fit, field structure,
  or causal exclusion.

### Method composition is a measured boundary condition, not a complete explanation

- **Source claim:** Intersectionality's frequent use of qualitative methods is
  one route through which topic and method can jointly limit entry into
  mainstream psychology journals (pp. 802–803).
- **Evidence:** Of the 1,642 retrieved articles, 441 (27%) were classified as
  qualitative; only 1.6% of those qualitative intersectionality articles were
  placed in the 30 highest-impact psychology journals. For context, the authors
  cite Eagly and Riger's estimate that 8.7% of psychology empirical articles
  between 2004 and 2012 used only qualitative methods, falling to 1.8% among
  30 high-impact journals (pp. 802–803).
- **Locator:** “Evidence for the formal epistemic exclusion of Intersectionality
  theory,” pp. 802–803.
- **Caveat:** The focal paper does not report how it classified methods or model
  topic and method simultaneously. Methodological fit could mediate part of the
  outlet pattern, but the evidence does not determine whether the standards are
  epistemically biased, appropriate for particular claims, or both in different
  cases.

### Funding evidence is relevant but imported and not intersectionality-specific

- **Source claim:** Formal evaluation can combine bias about scholars with bias
  about the research topics that marginalized scholars more often pursue,
  contributing to lower funding success (p. 803).
- **Evidence:** The paper cites studies of racial and gender differences in NIH
  R01 awards and highlights Hoppe et al.'s finding that Black applicants more
  often proposed work on health disparities, prevention, intervention, or
  marginalized groups and that these topics had lower award rates (p. 803).
- **Locator:** “Evidence for the formal epistemic exclusion of Intersectionality
  theory,” p. 803.
- **Caveat:** The focal article conducts no grant analysis, and the cited results
  concern biomedical topics and applicant groups rather than intersectionality
  proposals specifically. They support the plausibility of topic-mediated
  evaluation but do not directly demonstrate that psychology funders exclude
  intersectionality or isolate evaluator prejudice from topic, institute,
  method, prior-resource, or application-quality differences.

### Informal exclusion is evidenced through prior studies, not newly observed gatekeeper behavior

- **Source claim:** Informal epistemic exclusion appears as lack of recognition,
  assertions that intersectionality is irrelevant or illegitimate, and claims
  that it is incomprehensible; repeated shallow or inaccurate applications
  also make the theory visible as a buzzword while obscuring its core commitments
  (pp. 803–805).
- **Evidence:** The article draws on interviews with faculty of color who
  reported denied recognition and questioned scholarly legitimacy, published
  critiques of psychology's treatment of intersectionality, and a content
  analysis finding that counseling-psychology articles often addressed
  intersecting identities without analyzing interlocking power or calling for
  social-justice action. These sources are used to interpret repeated
  misrepresentation and stated disciplinary non-fit (pp. 799–805).
- **Locator:** “How epistemic exclusion is expressed,” pp. 799–800; “Evidence
  for the informal epistemic exclusion of Intersectionality theory,”
  pp. 803–804; “Visibility theory and the epistemic exclusion of
  intersectionality,” pp. 804–805.
- **Caveat:** The evidence layers differ. The faculty interviews concern
  epistemic exclusion generally, the content analysis assesses applications
  rather than evaluator intent, and several examples are scholarly observations
  or interpretations. The focal paper does not systematically sample reviews,
  editorial letters, conference decisions, or statements by disciplinary
  gatekeepers.

### Visibility theory specifies three proposed power processes

- **Source claim:** Power differences allow dominant psychologists to restrict
  intersectionality's access to journals, grants, and institutions; distort it
  through superficial or inaccurate use; and reinforce boundaries that preserve
  the disciplinary status quo (p. 805).
- **Evidence:** The authors map the publication, funding, recognition, and
  misrepresentation evidence discussed earlier onto three visibility processes:
  unequal gatekeeping power, distorted recognition that combines
  hypervisibility and invisibility, and maintenance of social boundaries and
  hierarchy (pp. 804–805).
- **Locator:** “Visibility theory and the epistemic exclusion of
  intersectionality,” pp. 804–805.
- **Caveat:** This is a theoretical synthesis rather than a separately tested
  causal model. The article does not observe gatekeeper motives or show that
  successful dominant psychologists resist intersectionality to protect their
  own status; those interests are plausible interpretations, not measured
  findings.

### Proposed remedies follow the structural framing but are not evaluated interventions

- **Source claim:** Epistemic inclusion requires changing who holds editorial
  power, broadening journal policies and accepted methods, expanding psychology
  training, and valuing both existing intersectionality venues and integration
  into the disciplinary center (p. 808).
- **Evidence:** The recommendations correspond to the proposed mechanisms:
  include intersectionality experts on editorial boards, admit mixed methods,
  participatory action research, and critical ethnography, and teach a wider
  range of epistemologies and methods (p. 808).
- **Locator:** “Ways that psychology can promote the epistemic inclusion of
  intersectionality,” p. 808.
- **Caveat:** These are normative recommendations. The paper does not compare
  interventions or show which change improves publication, understanding,
  scholarly recognition, or career outcomes.

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- The article has no conventional limitations section and reports no
  limitations for its PsycINFO search. It does state that the authors'
  positionalities and insider experiences shape their approach and that they
  use discussion, engagement with other scholarship, and self-reflection to
  address resulting limits (pp. 797–798).
- The authors acknowledge diverse interpretations and extensions of
  intersectionality. Their assessment of accurate representation is anchored
  in a declared boundary: an application must contain three core tenets—mutually
  constitutive inequalities, socially situated identities, and a relation
  between theory and social-justice praxis—to fully represent the theory
  (pp. 798, 800).
- Some evidence is explicitly described as emerging, and several proposed
  consequences for scholars are framed as possibilities rather than measured
  outcomes (pp. 803, 807).

### Reviewer assessment

- The DOI, title, author order, year, venue, volume, issue, and page range match
  Crossref, Semantic Scholar, OpenAlex, the Wiley record, and the 18-page
  published text deposited in Deep Blue. The article was published by Wiley
  for the Society for the Psychological Study of Social Issues, and the authors
  declared no known conflict of interest (p. 796). No retraction or corrected
  bibliographic identity was identified in those records as reviewed on
  2026-07-20.
- The source's central contribution is conceptual. For its causal explanation
  of intersectionality's exclusion, it is best treated as Level VII evidence,
  with a Level VI descriptive bibliographic component. Overall Grade C is
  appropriate: useful framing and source-specific distributional evidence,
  but insufficient on its own for causal or field-wide claims.
- The PsycINFO check is not reproducible from the reported information. A
  single search term may miss work that applies intersectional reasoning
  without using that label and may include superficial uses the authors
  themselves regard as misrepresentation.
- Outlet concentration is not equivalent to invisibility, rejection, or
  devaluation. Specialty and interdisciplinary journals may be chosen for
  audience fit; top-impact journal shares also depend on article volume,
  subfield, method, publication age, and author submission behavior.
- The article does not provide a comparator theory, an overall psychology
  baseline, submission records, acceptance decisions, reviewer reports, or a
  model separating topic, method, author identity, and venue effects. It
  therefore cannot estimate whether disciplinary standards, identity bias,
  self-selection, specialization, or unequal opportunity accounts for the
  observed distribution.
- The claim that marginalized scholars disproportionately use
  intersectionality and that stereotypes about them spill over to evaluations
  of the theory is plausible and grounded in related literature, but it is not
  directly tested in the focal article.
- The three visibility processes clarify a possible mechanism, but motives such
  as gatekeepers protecting their status remain inferred. Structural
  reproduction does not require conscious prejudice, yet the paper also lacks
  evidence that would distinguish unconscious norm enforcement from
  defensible claim-specific quality judgments.
- The study concerns a specific theory within psychology and includes a U.S.
  funding example. It should not be generalized to journal, country,
  institution, article, author, or community visibility across all fields
  without additional evidence.

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** A clear conceptual mechanism linking disciplinary norms,
  identity-conditioned credibility, formal evaluation, informal recognition,
  and power over accurate visibility. It also provides descriptive evidence
  that intersectionality articles—and especially qualitative ones—were rarely
  placed in the highest-impact psychology journals in the authors' 2004–2019
  PsycINFO retrieval.
- **Project interpretation:** Later synthesis can treat epistemic exclusion as
  a candidate explanation for scholarly devaluation at the level of a theory,
  research program, and its scholars. The evidence record should keep outlet
  placement, funding, accurate representation, recognition, and career
  consequences as distinct outcomes and label whether support is focal,
  imported, or theoretical.
- **Does not establish:** That editorial gatekeepers rejected the retrieved
  articles; that the publication pattern was caused by author identity or
  discriminatory intent; that intersectionality work is equal in quality to
  every mainstream alternative; that specialty-journal placement means no
  audience or impact; the size of any causal effect; or that the same mechanism
  explains selective-index exclusion, citation inequality, country-level
  visibility, or journal survival. It does not grant this note or later
  exploratory work Paper Analysis authority.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Epistemic exclusion; disciplinary legitimacy; formal and informal
  gatekeeping; topic and method evaluation; identity-conditioned scholarly
  credibility; invisibility and hypervisibility; theory misrepresentation;
  publication and funding hierarchies.
- **Agreements or tensions:** The source complements Alperin and Rozemblum's
  formal policy account by showing how field-internal definitions of rigor and
  mainstream outlets may operate as legitimacy gates. It differs from Khanna
  et al.'s infrastructural journal-discoverability problem because the focal
  outcome is a theory's disciplinary recognition and accurate representation.
  Larivière et al.'s aggregate citation-dispersion countertrend remains
  compatible because Settles et al. neither analyze citations nor establish a
  universal concentration trend.

## Open questions

- Can the PsycINFO analysis be reproduced with a documented query, journal-rank
  source, screening protocol, article-level data, and sensitivity checks using
  related intersectionality terms?
- Relative to all psychology articles and to comparable theories, how unusual
  is the observed top-journal placement after accounting for subfield, method,
  article age, and output volume?
- Do submission, reviewer, and editorial-decision records show that topic,
  method, author identity, or their interaction changes acceptance when
  manuscript quality is held constant?
- How often do mainstream articles omit each of the three declared tenets, and
  how sensitive is a misrepresentation finding to competing definitions of
  intersectionality?
- Which evidence can distinguish exclusion from journal specialization,
  audience choice, methodological fit, time lag, and author self-selection?
- Do editorial-board representation, broader method policies, or training
  changes measurably improve accurate representation, publication placement,
  funding, recognition, belonging, or career outcomes?
