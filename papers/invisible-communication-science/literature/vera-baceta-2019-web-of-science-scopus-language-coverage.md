# Vera-Baceta et al. (2019) — Web of Science and Scopus language coverage

- **Citation:** Vera-Baceta, M.-A., Thelwall, M., & Kousha, K. (2019). Web of Science and Scopus language coverage. *Scientometrics, 121*(3), 1803–1813. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11192-019-03264-z
- **Zotero:** [Open source item in Zotero](zotero://select/library/items/AJXHMGS9)
- **DOI or URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s11192-019-03264-z
- **Reviewed:** 2026-07-20
- **Reading basis:** Full author-submitted manuscript, article pp. 1–11, from the University of Wolverhampton repository

## Why this source matters

Vera-Baceta et al. provide a document-level comparison of the languages and
research areas represented in Web of Science and Scopus during 2018. The study
is useful because it shows that an aggregate statement such as “Scopus has
broader coverage” can reverse for particular language–field combinations. It
belongs in the corpus as evidence about index-specific discoverability, with a
firm boundary: database record counts are not measures of recognition,
prestige, citation circulation, or the total amount of scholarship produced in
a language.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Determine how many documents Web of Science and Scopus
  index by language and research area, using document-level rather than
  journal-level data (Research question and Methods, article pp. 2–3).
- **Conceptual framing:** Research evaluation depends on representative source
  coverage. Language and field composition therefore matter when Web of
  Science or Scopus is used to evaluate non-English-speaking countries. The
  paper distinguishes database coverage from the wider scientific activity
  that an evaluation is intended to represent (Introduction, article pp. 1–2).
- **Contribution claimed:** A disaggregated language-by-research-area analysis
  gives more evaluation-relevant information than journal counts or aggregate
  document totals and identifies cases in which Web of Science, rather than
  Scopus, is the more comprehensive of the two sources (Conclusions, article p.
  10).

## Methods

- **Design:** Cross-sectional descriptive comparison of the two databases'
  indexed-document counts for a single publication year (Methods, article pp.
  2–3).
- **Measures or classifications:** Coverage is explicitly operationalized as
  the number of documents indexed in each database. Language comes from the
  databases' language fields. The authors map 152 Web of Science Research Areas
  and 28 Scopus Subject Areas into five broad Web of Science-derived groups:
  Arts & Humanities, Life Sciences & Biomedicine, Physical Sciences, Social
  Sciences, and Technology (Research question; Subjects, article pp. 2–3).
- **Analysis:** The study compares counts and proportions by database,
  language, broad research area, and language–area combination. Documents can
  have more than one language or subject classification, so category totals can
  exceed database totals. English and non-English records are also analysed
  separately (Languages and Subjects, article p. 3; Results, article pp. 4–8).
- **Validation or robustness:** All counts were collected on the same date to
  limit asynchronous database change. The authors report that an initial 2017
  extraction produced similar results, and they use language–field
  disaggregation to test whether aggregate patterns persist. No external
  reference corpus, document-level matching between databases, query strings,
  or validation of language and subject tags is reported (Methods, article pp.
  2–3; Discussion, article p. 9).

## Findings

### English dominates both indexes, but the indexed share is not a production denominator

- **Source claim:** English is the dominant indexed language in both databases,
  with a higher English share in Web of Science than in Scopus (Language
  coverage, article p. 4).
- **Evidence:** English accounts for 92.64% of Scopus documents and 95.37% of
  Web of Science documents. The next-largest language is Chinese in Scopus at
  2.76% and Spanish in Web of Science at 1.26% (Language coverage, article p.
  4).
- **Locator:** Results, “Language coverage,” article p. 4.
- **Caveat:** These percentages use each database as its own denominator. They
  do not estimate the share of worldwide scholarly production in English or
  the probability that a document in a given language is indexed.

### Scopus has more non-English records overall, with substantial language-specific reversals

- **Source claim:** Scopus' main aggregate advantage over Web of Science is its
  larger non-English document count, but it is not more comprehensive for every
  language (Language coverage, article p. 4).
- **Evidence:** Scopus contains about 25% more non-English documents but only
  about 2% more English documents. It indexes 86,821 Chinese documents compared
  with 7,231 in Web of Science, while Web of Science indexes more Spanish
  documents (37,113 versus 30,632) and Portuguese documents (12,560 versus
  11,734). Catalan, Croatian, Italian, Malay, Norwegian, and Turkish also reverse
  the aggregate Scopus advantage (Language coverage and Figure 1, article pp.
  4–5).
- **Locator:** Results, “Language coverage,” and Figure 1, article pp. 4–5.
- **Caveat:** A larger count may reflect database scope, indexed journal supply,
  document-type mix, subject composition, or tagging differences. The design
  does not identify which mechanism produced a language difference.

### Language-by-field disaggregation changes the database comparison

- **Source claim:** Aggregate language or field totals can conceal which
  database has greater coverage for a specific language–research-area pair
  (Discussion, article pp. 8–9).
- **Evidence:** Web of Science has more Spanish documents overall, yet Scopus
  has more Spanish documents in Life Sciences & Biomedicine, Physical Sciences,
  and Technology. Conversely, Scopus has a much larger Life Sciences &
  Biomedicine total, but Web of Science has greater coverage for Norwegian and
  Turkish within that area. Table 1 reports the complete matrix (Table 1 and
  Discussion, article pp. 7–9).
- **Locator:** Table 1, article pp. 7–8; Discussion, article pp. 8–9.
- **Caveat:** The five broad areas are the authors' crosswalk from two different
  classification systems, and a document can enter multiple areas. The crosswalk
  makes comparison possible but does not make the original taxonomies
  equivalent.

### Index-policy change is a visible temporal confound

- **Source claim:** Earlier comparisons may no longer describe Web of Science
  because the Core Collection expanded with the Emerging Sources Citation Index
  in 2015 (Discussion, article p. 9).
- **Evidence:** The paper reports jumps in Web of Science records from 2014 to
  2015 for Spanish (15,101 to 39,504), Portuguese (5,628 to 14,685), Russian
  (3,584 to 13,665), Italian (4,259 to 7,587), and Turkish (1,742 to 4,308)
  (Figures 5–6 and Discussion, article p. 9).
- **Locator:** Figures 5–6 and Discussion, article p. 9.
- **Caveat:** The comparison is descriptive. It does not separate the ESCI
  addition from contemporaneous changes in journal publishing, database
  backfilling, document types, or language metadata.

### Small language–field combinations remain nearly absent from both sources

- **Source claim:** Some retained languages still have research areas with very
  low combined representation across both databases (Coverage problems,
  article pp. 9–10).
- **Evidence:** Using a threshold of 91 combined records, the paper identifies
  sparse combinations including Norwegian Physical Sciences and Technology
  (zero records in both), Slovenian Physical Sciences (zero), Swedish Physical
  Sciences (zero), and Korean Arts & Humanities (70 combined records), among
  others (Coverage problems, article pp. 9–10).
- **Locator:** “Coverage problems,” article pp. 9–10.
- **Caveat:** Low indexed counts do not show whether relevant scholarship was
  produced but excluded, published under a different language tag or field,
  represented in other document types, or genuinely scarce.

### The paper tests aggregation effects, not a causal explanation for linguistic selectivity

- **Source claim:** The authors infer that Web of Science has emphasized Arts &
  Humanities and major Latin American languages, whereas Scopus has emphasized
  Technology, Life Sciences & Biomedicine, and several Asian languages
  (Discussion, article p. 9).
- **Evidence:** The inference rests on the observed language–field count matrix,
  including Web of Science advantages in Arts & Humanities for many languages
  and Scopus advantages in Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Technology, and Life
  Sciences & Biomedicine (Table 1 and Discussion, article pp. 7–9).
- **Locator:** Table 1, article pp. 7–8; Discussion, article p. 9.
- **Caveat:** No selection-policy data, eligible-journal denominator, editorial
  criteria, author behavior, audience size, publication volume, or causal model
  is analysed. “Effort” and “attention” are interpretations of coverage
  patterns, not tested mechanisms.

### Coverage volume is explicitly not treated as quality or recognition

- **Source claim:** The authors question whether document volume alone is enough
  to judge a database adequate and state that other quality factors may matter
  (Discussion, article p. 9).
- **Evidence:** They acknowledge that they did not check whether records in
  either database were incorrect, and the conclusion recommends considering
  both document and journal coverage when choosing a source (Discussion,
  article p. 9; Conclusions, article p. 10).
- **Locator:** Discussion, article p. 9; Conclusions, article p. 10.
- **Caveat:** The study does not operationalize quality, recognition, citation
  impact, local use, prestige, or evaluation consequences. It therefore cannot
  rank the scholarly value of indexed or non-indexed work.

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- Language counts can exceed database totals because some documents are tagged
  with multiple languages, either correctly or by mistake; the excess is at
  most 0.43% (Languages, article p. 3).
- Twenty-six languages below the 0.01% threshold are excluded from the main
  analysis, although the paper reports their combined record count (Languages,
  article p. 3).
- Subject totals exceed document totals because documents can be classified in
  more than one area, and some records lack subject or language information
  (Subjects, article p. 3).
- The authors did not check whether either database contained incorrect records
  or whether volume alone was an adequate quality indicator (Discussion,
  article p. 9).

### Reviewer assessment

- The study compares each index with the other rather than with an external
  population of eligible scholarship. It measures relative indexed volume, not
  recall, exclusion probability, or language selectivity against actual output.
- Counts are obtained through aggregate interface queries rather than
  document-level matching. The analysis therefore cannot distinguish unique
  coverage, overlap, duplicates, or differently classified versions of the
  same work.
- Database language tags and the subject crosswalk are not independently
  validated. Field composition is partly exposed by disaggregation, but
  document type, country, journal age, publisher, and publication volume remain
  untested confounds.
- The abstract reports 6,094,079 analysed documents while Methods reports
  6,071,821. The discrepancy should be resolved before reusing a total sample
  size (Abstract; Methods, article p. 2).
- The author-submitted manuscript is openly available under CC BY-NC-ND, while
  the publisher page provides only a subscription preview. Exact quotations or
  pagination-sensitive reuse should be checked against the version of record.

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** Treating Web of Science and Scopus as different,
  time-specific discovery environments whose language coverage varies by field;
  disaggregating language and research area before making a coverage claim; and
  recognizing that one index's aggregate advantage does not hold for every
  language–field combination.
- **Project interpretation:** The paper supplies a direct measurement precedent
  for index-specific linguistic discoverability. In the conceptual map, it is
  evidence that index construction can create uneven visibility, but only at
  the level of database inclusion. A later explanatory account still needs an
  external publication denominator and evidence about selection policy,
  infrastructure, author behavior, language networks, and evaluation systems.
- **Does not establish:** That English-language scholarship receives more
  epistemic recognition; that non-English scholarship is rejected because of
  language; that indexed counts reflect quality, citations, prestige, audience,
  policy use, or total production; that either database is currently more
  comprehensive; or that database inclusion causes recognition.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Linguistic selectivity of bibliographic indexes; database coverage
  as discoverability; field composition and aggregation; research-evaluation
  validity; temporal change in index scope; separation of visibility from
  recognition.
- **Agreements or tensions:** The paper agrees with Khanna et al. that selective
  indexes cannot be assumed to represent all scholarly activity. Unlike Khanna
  et al.'s OJS study, however, it has no external journal or publication
  population against which to estimate database coverage. Its language
  differences are therefore comparative index counts, not direct evidence of
  exclusion from a known corpus.

## Open questions

- What explains the difference between the abstract's 6,094,079 documents and
  the Methods section's 6,071,821?
- How would language-specific recall change if national indexes, Crossref,
  OpenAlex, or publisher registries supplied an external denominator?
- How much of each language difference remains after document type, field,
  journal age, publisher country, publication volume, and database overlap are
  controlled?
- Are the database language and subject tags accurate for multilingual or
  multidisciplinary documents?
- Have later database expansions changed the 2018 language–field pattern?
- Through what evidence, if any, does index inclusion lead to citations,
  evaluation rewards, prestige, policy use, or other forms of recognition?
