# Wu (2024) — Gender citation gap

- **Citation:** Wu, C. (2024). The gender citation gap: Approaches,
  explanations, and implications. *Sociology Compass, 18*(2), e13189.
  https://doi.org/10.1111/soc4.13189
- **Zotero:** [Open source item in Zotero](zotero://select/library/items/IN3Z4IM3)
- **DOI or URL:** https://doi.org/10.1111/soc4.13189
- **Reviewed:** 2026-07-20
- **Reading basis:** Full text, publisher-provided Version of Record, pp. 1–18

## Why this source matters

Wu separates three measurements that are often called the same “gender
citation gap”: citations per article, citations accumulated per author, and the
gender composition of reference lists relative to an eligible pool. This makes
the review a useful counter-reading for the present corpus. It argues that
women's lower career citation totals can arise mainly from fewer publications
over a career even when each published article receives a comparable citation
rate, while retaining field-specific evidence of gendered citation selection.

## Research question and framing

- **Question or aim:** Review whether women face a citation disadvantage, show
  how the answer changes across three approaches, explain the resulting gaps,
  and discuss possible career consequences (Abstract; Introduction, pp. 1–2).
- **Conceptual framing:** The article distinguishes an article-level outcome
  (citations to a paper), an author-level outcome (citations accumulated during
  a period or career), and a reference-ratio outcome (the observed gender
  composition of a reference list compared with the composition of a pool of
  potentially citable work). It then separates explanations about evaluation
  and citing behaviour from explanations about career-long publication output
  (Sections 2–3, pp. 2–11).
- **Contribution claimed:** Once units and denominators are separated, the
  author argues that the weight of evidence shows little average per-article
  penalty but a persistent author-level gap, making women's lower career
  publication output a central explanation for lower accumulated citations
  (Introduction, pp. 1–2; Sections 3.2 and 5, pp. 10–13).

## Methods

- **Design:** Narrative review organized around three measurement approaches
  and two explanatory levels (Sections 2–3, pp. 2–11).
- **Measures or classifications:** “Per-article” compares citations to papers
  classified by the gender of a dominant author; “per-author” compares total or
  period-average citations of scholars; “reference-ratio” compares observed
  reference-list composition with the gender composition of an eligible source
  pool (Sections 2.1–2.3, pp. 2–9).
- **Analysis:** The author groups prior findings by approach, compares their
  directions, then narratively assesses undervaluation, representation,
  homophily, self-citation, publication output, collaboration, specialization,
  care and service work, career length, rank, and resources as explanations
  (Sections 2–3, pp. 2–11).
- **Validation or robustness:** Contrary findings are retained in Table 1 and
  the prose. No reproducible search, inclusion and exclusion process, study-
  quality appraisal, effect-size synthesis, sensitivity analysis, or formal
  test of publication bias is reported.

## Findings

### The three approaches answer different questions

- **Source claim:** The apparent direction of the gender citation gap depends
  on the unit and denominator used (Sections 2 and 5, pp. 2–13).
- **Evidence:** Per-article studies ask whether papers by women and men receive
  different citation rates. Per-author studies aggregate citations across a
  period or career. Reference-ratio studies compare observed citations to
  gendered authorship shares in a pool of work that could have been cited
  (Sections 2.1–2.3, pp. 2–9). Wu reports that per-author studies consistently
  show women accumulating fewer citations, whereas a substantial body of
  per-article and reference-ratio work reports parity or an advantage for women
  (Section 2.3, p. 9).
- **Locator:** Sections 2.1–2.3 and Table 1, pp. 2–9; Conclusion, pp. 12–13.
- **Caveat:** Reference-ratio studies do not estimate the same outcome as
  per-article studies. A field can under-cite women relative to its eligible
  publication pool while the subset of women-authored papers that is cited
  receives an average rate comparable to men's papers.

### Average per-article parity coexists with important contrary findings

- **Source claim:** The weight of reviewed evidence does not support a general
  penalty to women-authored articles, but the direction varies by field, period,
  model, and sample (Introduction, p. 2; Section 2.1 and Table 1, pp. 2–8).
- **Evidence:** The review contrasts broad parity or small female advantages in
  studies such as Thelwall (2020), Symonds et al. (2006), and Lynn et al.
  (2019) with penalties reported by Larivière et al. (2013), Mohammad (2020),
  Chatterjee and Werner (2021), Sebo and Clair (2023), and others. Table 1 also
  records significant reference-ratio gaps in anthropology, economics,
  neuroscience, communication, and physics (Table 1, pp. 3–8).
- **Locator:** Introduction and Section 2.1, p. 2; Table 1, pp. 3–8; Section
  2.3, p. 9.
- **Caveat:** The review does not weight studies formally by design quality or
  precision. Its conclusion about the “weight” of evidence is a narrative
  judgment, not a pooled estimate.

### Publication output is a strong composition explanation for career totals

- **Source claim:** Fewer publications over a career provide an apparent and
  central explanation for women's lower total citations even when citation
  impact is similar for bodies of work of the same size (Section 3.2, pp.
  10–11).
- **Evidence:** The review reports Huang et al.'s (2020) finding that active men
  and women publish at comparable annual rates and have equivalent career-wise
  impact for the same number of publications, but women have shorter careers,
  higher dropout, fewer total papers, and therefore fewer accumulated
  citations. It also reports that prior publication count reduces gender
  effects and that productive authors of any gender receive an initial
  self-citation visibility boost (Table 1, pp. 4 and 8; Section 3.2, pp.
  10–11).
- **Locator:** Table 1, pp. 4 and 8; Section 3.2, pp. 10–11; Conclusion, p. 12.
- **Caveat:** Wu does not perform a new decomposition showing how much of the
  career-level citation gap publication count explains across fields. “Primary
  reason” is therefore the review's interpretation of heterogeneous studies,
  not a universally estimated causal share.

### Lower output is embedded in gendered career conditions

- **Source claim:** Publication output should not be read as an isolated author
  trait; it can transmit disadvantages in collaboration, specialization,
  service and family responsibilities, career duration, rank, networks, and
  access to resources into citation totals (Section 3.2, pp. 11–12).
- **Evidence:** Wu links collaboration to total publication count, describes
  how shorter or disrupted careers miss cumulative self-citation visibility,
  and summarizes evidence relating family and service responsibilities,
  restricted networks, lower rank, fewer resources, and topic choices to
  productivity and impact (Section 3.2, pp. 10–12).
- **Locator:** Section 3.2, pp. 10–12.
- **Caveat:** These mechanisms come from different studies and are not tested
  together in the review. Some may affect both output and per-paper recognition,
  and citations can themselves feed back into rank, resources, collaboration,
  and later productivity.

### Citation-selection bias remains a live, outcome-specific explanation

- **Source claim:** Evidence for undervaluation, homophily, representation, and
  self-citation is mixed and often contradictory at the article level, so these
  mechanisms should not automatically be used to explain aggregate career
  totals (Section 3.1, pp. 9–10).
- **Evidence:** Dworkin et al. (2020) found neuroscience reference lists cited
  more men-led papers than expected after excluding self-citations; Wang et al.
  (2021) and Teich et al. (2022) also reported gendered reference patterns.
  Mohammad (2020) and several medical studies reported per-article penalties.
  Conversely, replication and controlled studies cited by Wu found small, null,
  or reversed differences, and Mishra et al. (2018) found gender had the weakest
  effect on self-citation probability among the modeled features (Table 1, pp.
  3–8; Sections 2.3 and 3.1, pp. 9–10).
- **Locator:** Table 1, pp. 3–8; Sections 2.3 and 3.1, pp. 9–10.
- **Caveat:** Mixed findings prevent both a universal citation-bias claim and a
  universal no-bias claim. The appropriate conclusion is conditional on field,
  citation outcome, author-position rule, eligible reference pool, controls,
  and observation window.

### Career consequences are plausible but not established by this review

- **Source claim:** Lower accumulated citations may reduce women's visibility,
  recognition, funding, promotion, tenure, salary, and the circulation of their
  ideas (Section 4, pp. 11–12).
- **Evidence:** Wu connects citations to evaluative decisions through prior
  literature and reports an association in the author's earlier Canadian study
  between citations and salary (Section 4, pp. 11–12).
- **Locator:** Sections 4.1–4.2, pp. 11–12.
- **Caveat:** The author explicitly says empirical research on these downstream
  implications is limited (pp. 2 and 11). The review does not establish that
  lower citations cause any specific career decision or visibility outcome.

## Limitations

### Source-reported limitations

- Table 1 is “not meant to be an all-inclusive compilation” of published
  studies (Section 2, p. 2).
- Empirical research on how citation gaps affect visibility and other career
  inequalities is limited, and the discussion is intended to motivate future
  work (Introduction, p. 2; Section 4, p. 11).
- The conclusion calls for research on intersectional citation patterns
  involving race, gender, geography, and other social positions (Conclusion,
  p. 13).
- The author declares no conflict of interest (p. 13).

### Reviewer assessment

- Under the Academic Research Suite hierarchy, this is Level VII narrative-
  review evidence and overall Grade C for the claim that publication-output
  composition is the dominant explanation. It is useful for conceptual
  differentiation and source discovery, but its non-systematic search and lack
  of study-quality weighting or meta-analysis do not support a definitive
  across-field causal ranking.
- The conclusion that women's research is not cited less “at the reference
  level” is broader than the review's own Table 1, which contains multiple
  significant reference-ratio gaps. The safer synthesis is that findings are
  heterogeneous and that per-author totals cannot identify a per-paper or
  reference-selection mechanism by themselves.
- Publication count, active annual rate, career length, attrition, collaboration,
  and cumulative citation totals are distinct constructs. Calling all of them
  “productivity” can hide whether a gap reflects pace while active, duration of
  participation, team structure, access to resources, or citation feedback.
- Gender classification varies across the reviewed studies, and the review does
  not provide a cross-study assessment of binary inference, missingness,
  misclassification, author-position conventions, or intersectional validity.
- The review relies in part on Wu's own 2023 studies when advancing the career-
  course account. This is not a declared financial conflict, but it makes
  independent replication and evidence weighting especially important.
- DOI, title, author, journal, volume, issue, year, open-access license, and
  Version of Record identity were verified against Crossref, OpenAlex, Zotero,
  and the complete publisher-provided full text. Scite reported no contrasting
  citation statement at review time, but that index signal is not a substitute
  for independent appraisal.

## Project relevance

- **Source supports:** Keeping article-, author-, and reference-list outcomes
  separate; treating publication-output composition and career duration as a
  serious competing explanation for author-level citation inequality; and
  retaining field-specific citation-selection bias rather than assuming one
  universal direction.
- **Project interpretation:** Uneven scholarly visibility can be generated
  cumulatively without a per-paper penalty: fewer publications, shorter careers,
  disrupted networks, and lower access to resources reduce the number of
  opportunities for citation and recognition. This is a composition and career-
  process pathway that can coexist with gendered selection in particular
  reference lists or fields.
- **Does not establish:** That gender bias in citing is absent; that publication
  output is the dominant explanation in every discipline or identity group;
  the causal share of any mechanism; equal visibility for women; index admission
  or discoverability effects; non-academic use; or relevance to the OJS journal
  population without further evidence. It does not grant this note or corpus
  Paper Analysis authority.

## Literature-review connections

- **Themes:** Units and denominators; article-level versus career-level citation
  outcomes; publication-output composition; attrition and career duration;
  cumulative advantage; self-citation; homophily; reference-list selection;
  gendered academic work and resources; competing explanations for visibility.
- **Agreements or tensions:** Wu's career-composition account can coexist with
  Dworkin et al.'s field-specific reference-ratio finding because they estimate
  different outcomes. It conflicts with any claim that aggregate author-level
  citation gaps alone demonstrate per-paper undervaluation, but it does not
  refute studies that directly estimate gendered citation selection within a
  defined publication pool.

## Open questions

- In matched longitudinal data, how much of the author-level citation gap is
  separately attributable to publication pace while active, career duration,
  attrition, collaboration, field and journal placement, self-citation, and
  per-paper citation rates?
- When reference-ratio and per-article outcomes are calculated in the same
  field, cohort, and eligible publication pool, under what conditions can
  reference-list undercitation coexist with average per-paper parity?
- Do the career-composition results hold for nonbinary scholars and at the
  intersections of gender with race, geography, language, disability, class,
  institution, and academic rank?
- How much of the association between publication output and citation totals is
  reciprocal cumulative advantage, in which earlier citations improve later
  resources, networks, retention, and publication opportunities?
